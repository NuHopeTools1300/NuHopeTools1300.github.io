Post #46  (forum post #651895)
Author    : star-art
Date      : Aug 17, 2008 at 4:45 PM
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

For me, I wouldn't bother. The cast part is still likely to suffer heat damage and warp. Epoxy is heat sensitive and I am told it can be damaged by too much heat. Polyester is better at standing up to heat, but it stinks to high heaven, is more toxic, and is more brittle.

If you use LEDs instead, you can get a blinding light out of your engine bells with little or no heat. Many people think the LED light is too blue (which is very true), but you can now get warmer white LEDs that are made to look like halogen light. So, for me, that's what I'd use.

Or, the blue-white color of a standard white LED could be considered perfect for the engines as they added an animated blue-white flame on film anyway (at least in ANH).

When measuring from photos, all it takes is being off 1% to turn into a 6+ inch difference by the time you get from one end of a large model to the other. I've spent hundreds of hours dealing with this sort of thing for many different projects. Getting the proportions from photos is hard enough due to perspective distortion. Yet, scaling to fit the parts can sometimes be just as tricky -- or even harder.

Based on experience, I would not decide on the length by measuring and comparing just one part. A better approach is to do this with a half dozen or more parts. When you have a best guess as to the overall size, build a mockup. Then, test fit a bunch of different parts and see what you get. If they ALL appear to fit well, then you know you are really close.

Just keep in mind, a part can sometimes appear to fit even if your scaling is off 5 or 10 percent (or even more). So, that one part might look OK, but you might still run into problems with another part. That's why it's a good idea to pick your benchmark parts very carefully, and be sure you choose several different areas of the ship for your comparison. 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]

Good parts to check on this model include just about everything on the bridge, the L'Eggs eggs (keep in mind, those eggs look like they were trimmed and placed inside a tube, so they appear smaller than they really are), the 8RAD part of course, the mixing cups used as secondary engines, and a number of different parts on the superstructure.

There's a little parallax in that image as the domes are slightly farther away from the camera. The outside edge of each dome (the area you perceive as the edge of a circle) is farthest away, so it will appear slightly smaller than it really is in relation to the parts on the face of the bridge that are closer. Perhaps not much, but maybe enough to make a noticeable difference. . .

The amount of difference depends on the length of the lens. A wide angle lens will exaggerate such things the most.

The real trick when measuring from photos is using parts that are the same distance from the camera relative to each other, and making sure the angle relative to the camera is not such that perspective distortion gets in the way.

Hope that helps!

Very cool! This is similar to the technique I use, but probably more precise. I make a best guess as to dimensions and shape, then build a 3D model. I pose that from at least three different angles and see how well it matches the photos. I try to match 10 different photos/angles if I can. 

That can be a very difficult and subjective process because the shape, angle, distance from the camera, and focal length of the lens can all wreak havoc with the appearance of an object in a photo. But, if I get a very close match from at least three different angles, I know I have it almost dead on.

As to getting it close to begin with, what I do is scan key parts and make a 3D template from them, then apply those to my 3D model to check for fit. This turns the parts into a 3D "measuring stick" of sorts that helps determine at least the rough dimensions. After spending years trying to do all this in 2D, I've found the 3D approach is about 10 times faster. And, lots more accurate of course.

star-art said:

			Very cool! This is similar to the technique I use, but probably more precise. I make a best guess as to dimensions and shape, then build a 3D model. I pose that from at least three different angles and see how well it matches the photos. I try to match 10 different photos/angles if I can. 

That can be a very difficult and subjective process because the shape, angle, distance from the camera, and focal length of the lens can all wreak havoc with the appearance of an object in a photo. But, if I get a very close match from at least three different angles, I know I have it almost dead on.

As to getting it close to begin with, what I do is scan key parts and make a 3D template from them, then apply those to my 3D model to check for fit. This turns the parts into a 3D "measuring stick" of sorts that helps determine at least the rough dimensions. After spending years trying to do all this in 2D, I've found the 3D approach is about 10 times faster. And, lots more accurate of course.

Those both kits : Hasegawa 1/72 Morser Karl with Munitionspanzer

Hasegawa 1/72 54cm Morser Karl 041 wth Munitionspanzer IV Aus F dont have the conical part. 

What you need is the 60 cm version with the railway carrier (because in the kit with the panzer, you just have the Karl and the panzer, and most of the parts used on star wars stuff comes from the trucks which carry the Karl, yeah the Karl has treads and was selfproppeled but it was very slow so they carried it on railway for long distance). 

If you dont get the 60cm with railway carrier, you wont get the sprue on the last pic from studioscale.com. Since there are two identical trucks, you got twice the parts !
