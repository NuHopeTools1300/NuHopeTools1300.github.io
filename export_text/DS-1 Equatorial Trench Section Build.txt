Okay, here we go. The nerdiest yet coolest thing I’ve set out to build so far, and likely the most intensive. 

Like many others, I was obsessed with the Star Wars models since I knew how to use a glue bottle somewhat accurately. Specifically, the Death Star always had a unique place in my head. The scale and detailing that lies on the surface is impeccable, and provides an opportunity to explore some areas in even further detail. 

When I saw Rogue One in 2016 I was struck by this shot, where Krennic docks at the DS-1 through the equatorial trench. The last time we saw the equatorial trench as the audience was in Return of The Jedi, in which the DS-2’s equatorial trench was briefly shown under construction. For this project we are focusing on the DS-1 trench, and loosely basing our detailing on the shot below. The plan is to take a “slice” of this and build from there. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768710032766-png.2005027/]

I've been slowly working on a 3D model of my "slice" for years on and off now, and could just never figure out how I wanted to build it considering costs and appearance, but I've decided on a path now and have been making some smooth progress. 

One thing I have found as an architect/ ex-architecture student who crushed models in school, is that there's only so much you can model before it becomes redundant. Things will inevitably fit together terrible, or great, and 3D modeling assumes the best option. And, a hand-built aspect will only make a model feel more personal and loved rather than a machine produced model, I'll talk more about this below . I'm using my 3D model as inspiration at this point, to guide me through the general "theme" of certain areas while I detail them to further extents than what any 3D modeling could allow me to do in the same time frame. Below are the 3D model photos, for reference. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768710521755-png.2005028/]

I toyed with the notion of 3D printing this for a long time, and while it would've been easier technically, I don't have the budget or really the desire to do so. I've always been inspired by the OG Star Wars model production being handcrafted and kit-bashing based. I want to the same here, and hell, it's almost therapeutic man. So, this is/ will be entirely hand-built. I've built some of Randy Cooper's famous models, especially his Avenger Star Destroyer where he used clear cast resin pieces to be pre-lit from behind, then scratch off/ drill off paint where you want lighting to show. I thought about this, but with the risk of having light leaks and such I've decided that nothing other than our favorite fiber optic lighting will be necessary. This is a major component of how I will be building. 

To make things more complicated than they need to be, we are also going to be making this model a section cut of the equatorial trench. The goal is to add the interior of the DS-1 as best as we can, considering the scale of this model is 1:5000… insane. I’m loosely using this reference image for the interior detailing. We'll see how expansive this actually becomes in reality. I'd like for there to be at least a bit of the interior even if not as holistically as the reference photo shows. Time and sanity will tell. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768711202626-png.2005031/]

 

Considering that this model will need lighting via fiber optic wires, the surface of the DS-1 needs to be “solid,” or it cannot be 3D printed with any empty infill and it cannot be made in any way out of wood. The imaginary line from the surface to the rear needs to be clean, in order to get the 1mm fiber optic wire to fit smoothly in place. This might make more sense later. 

I have divided each large face of greebling/ detailing into more manageable sized panels. This will allow any low or hard to reach areas easier to detail. Our approach is to build a base panel, which will eventually be wrapped in styrene, to support the remaining panel details. Each panel, having varying heights and dimensions, will be cut out and glued to the surface of the base panel. This will allow us to save material and easily build more texture via different thicknesses of styrene. While the base panels do not need to be photo perfect, they absolutely need to be constructed with strong 90 degree corners and level faces. Big errors here will trickle up the model, literally, and yield us unlevel elements.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768710895641-jpeg.2005029/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768710905592-jpeg.2005030/]

Circling back to the goal of “solid” material to drill through, these boxes are hollow beneath so that the fiber optic cables can be organized by panel, and then by region. Fiber optic cables rely on a direct light source to one end of the wire, while the other end is installed on the finished side of the model, thus glowing in their 0.85mm space. The easier it is to organize and systematize these, the less headaches we’ll have down the road… ideally. In my mind, building the boxes out of cheap but easily workable PETG allows me to not waste more expensive styrene, and ensure it has a solid base to built on. It's definitely a pain in the rear to make these, but so far it has worked very well at keeping everything aligned on 90 degree angles. Makes it all look nice and level. I mean hell, one of my buddies thought this was 3D printed, so I'll take a compliment where I can get one!

Started the final section of what we're calling South, and got a good way through it. This section has been the most annoying so far, I think because of its straight width at ~9.50". Building the PETG box below was a pain because of the run of material. I had to reinforce it as best I could, and wrapping it with styrene was an equal in in my a**.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768800738037-jpeg.2005399/]

This exposed side of PETG is hosted in the "trench" section you'll see below. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768800800749-jpeg.2005400/]

This is the basic blocking. I try and migrate from "large" pieces of styrene and gradually get smaller and smaller. For reference, the pieces in the trench above ^ are around 5-10mm in width

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768800900233-jpeg.2005401/]

Remember that little ramekin if small pieces in my first post? There ya go, that's all of them. In addition to those, I cut some ~1mm strips to get the scale down a bit more. I knew this would be the most detailed portion of this southern section so I had to go all out. It's like an equatorial trench before the equatorial trench. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768801032193-jpeg.2005402/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768801512555-jpeg.2005404/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1768801055928-jpeg.2005403/]

Now I've gotta detail the top rim of this piece, drill some holes for lights and call it a day (for now) on the southern half. Then, we'll move on to what I'm calling "T.O. South," or the horizontal piece that will guide us into the actual equatorial trench. 

As much fun as it is detailing this and imagining what the surface of the DS-1 would look like at 1:5000 scale, I sometimes wonder if I'm overdoing it. We know that the real greebles of the Death Star kinda stop a fair distance before the trench, and then the paneling seems to smooth out a bit more. Thus, I wonder if I'm adding too much detail for a portion that seemingly is less greeble intensive. It's food for thought, and let me know what you think (not that there's really any going back now hahaha). 

With that said though, I could support my detailing by thinking about how the Death Star was built, specifically the automated droids they used in canon to construct it, I mean it's gotta be a complete mashup of panels and systems, thus making all of the surface greeble filled no?

Anyways, here's where I'm leaving off for the day! 

Cheers,

Finished this bottom section more or less, but I'll have a few photo-etched details to add here and there.

Been at that same struggle point I've come to a lot in thinking through this project, the internal support while also being a section cut. I know that this could be a model with a section cut, and I haven't completely ruled it out but it is incredibly difficult to plan for. While I decided to hand make a lot of this, a decision my wallet and I am thankful for, it makes blending 3D design and hand fabrication much more difficult.

Surprisingly, this lower piece comes in at an even 9.50" width by 10.125" height (enough evenness to work with in Fusion 360), there are still enough minute inconsistencies to make it difficult to 3D model around. If I'm going to 3D print something for interior support, it has to be nuts on, and I just know from experience that is easier said than done. Really, all of that is compounded by making it a section model.

So, my thoughts are to limit the secondary, interior "sectional".... sections. Basically create pockets where are most convenient for me, and aesthetically. For example, in the south, towards the rear we see a bit of the cavernous airways:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769487425747-png.2007143/]

And towards the center, maybe I adjust the position of the hangar bays so we can get a bit of this:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769487481664-png.2007144/]

It allows me to put further effort into everything else, while still getting a bit of "pop" on the sides for some added interest. Plus, those sections that show can take even more effort on my part, and for a good reason. Architect conflict compromise brain coming into effect here...

Still, how to efficiently support the interior of this increasingly large model is stressful. Hoping to work through some solutions this week. Let me know what y'all think of the reduced sectionality of the sides while I work on some tweaks.

Those curved wall beams could be done with blue foam: cut two exact curves in a Bristol paper (thinner than cardboard, but thicker than a regular sheet of paper).  Place those two curves on each side of your blue foam; take your hot wire and just follow the curves while cutting it.

Sand your foam with 180 grit and mold it in silicone to repeat all of the beams you need...my two cents worth.  Might be easier to 3-D print

Played in Fusion with some ideas for an SLA printed hangar bay. Basically, print it all in clear, but the angled protruding elements are separate. The "host" wall has notches that host the angled pieces. Since it would all be in clear, all the pieces could be printed clear and then either mask or scratch off black paint where it's not needed: 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769897867705-png.2008034/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769897936032-png.2008037/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769897884323-png.2008035/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769897911069-png.2008036/]

I used to own a Form 2 and while I know these could be printed and cleaned up from their supports well enough, keeping track of all of these printed pieces, paying for all of them, and just in general seems like a major pain in my a**.  I mean they're 1" tall the width, at its widest on the angled bits is 4mm. Seems doable, but risky. I would prefer to get an angled appearance like the movies, but tomorrow I am going to try hand building a few more versions, likely without any angled bits. I did a mock-up earlier this week and it has potential, albeit without the cool angled bits. Still thinking on this one.

In the meantime I've moved towards the center of the trench where all of the docks are. I plan to build the entire vertical face of the trench, then attach it to "T.O. South," the white base you'll see this piece on: 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769927941416-jpeg.2008164/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769927954407-jpeg.2008165/]

Because all of this needs paint, and I need to access the south side for lighting, and build a frame to support all of this, I've gotta keep a lot of this separate for now. It's annoying, but I continually check to make sure things are level (enough) to keep me happy. I think we all know building any kind of model is figuring out how to hide all your bull**** in often... creative ways. 

Started to make they real cool centerpiece, the recessed docking bays. The cutouts are not exactly perfect, but they're close. I made these "rough-ins" about 1/8" larger than they need to be. My thinking is since the hangars will be built separately, and will have about an 1/8 of extra sh** attached, I can then insert them into the rough-opening with room to spare, and hide the excess space on the front with detailing or a collar piece. Since their design isn't finalized, but I know what dimensions need to show, I feel confident with the spare 1/8" showing. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769928239422-jpeg.2008166/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1769928253636-jpeg.2008167/]

This box was an interesting one to build as it's an inverse of what I've been doing. Normally I can lather all of the corners up with glue, set a machinist square, and call it a day. Here, though, I can have glue in the corners. In the first pic of this you'll see some pieces of PETG sticking up behind the walls. Those are glued behind the hangar bay wall, that way I could adhere the walls to those instead of having glue show on the visible portions. It worked pretty well, I congratulated myself, and then had to rip out a wall as it wasn't at 90 degrees... such is life.

Still alive! And making some progress

Nothing to post quite yet, at least nothing that would look entirely different from my last post. 

Working on some hand-built hangars. I made a mock-up a few weeks ago but it was relatively **** compared to what I'm shooting for. 

If I hand-build them, it requires a lot of mm measuring, masking tape, black paint, and precision. 

Here's the thought:

First, mark the skinny (2mm) areas that light needs to get through on the interior surface (facing into the hangar):

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1772596040170-jpeg.2016791/]

Then, on the exterior side (facing into the model, hidden), mask off those 2mm areas that need light

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1772596096105-jpeg.2016792/]

Then paint the remaining space, 4mm, which on the interior side, will be covered by styrene (which is also painted black on the side that will be glued in place. Basically trying to block light where it isn't wanted, and have it shine through and reflect where I want. 

What will happen here, and I don't have the best pic (below) to show, is on the interior hangar side is we'll have the base 4mm plates glued and then within the 2mm "lit" spaces there will be another 4mm piece of styrene lifted above the gap. I've cut 1.5mm stubs, which are adhered to top and bottom of the wall, to raise that 4mm styrene bit above the base surface. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/1772596340819-png.2016793/]

From even my messy test ^^ it doesn't look bad. I think this new version will look better (don't have a good photo of how the light shines through). 

Yes, I keep with my mantra of hand-building all of it, but good Lord is this labor intensive. On the other hand, even if I 3D printed all of this like my previous post, it would require a similar amount of finishing and masking to get the same result. Ultimately, I'll likely go with the method above because clearly I hate myself. Just kidding, I live for this ****. 

As far as how to actually evenly light the area behind this is still up in the air. I figure if I could get some low lumen led strips behind with a diffuser it might work, but I really can't afford to lose more than 1/4" of space behind each wall. Let me know if y'all have any wild ideas, I'm open for them.

Peace!
