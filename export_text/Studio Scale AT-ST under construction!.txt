Hey Guys,

Vinyl guy aka (Dan L.) stopped by for a visit out here in Colorado and I put him to work!  Dan got his after some ESB ATST parts Isel just sent us in.  He did a great job!  Very detailed clay work on those pieces and Dan is a natural!

Thanks Dan!  ISel great parts!  Very easy to make into production parts!

[IMAGE: 281938012.jpg]

[IMAGE: 281938010.jpg]

While he was working on those I was getting the matrix mold ready for the ESB AT-ST head. The ESB head is inside the clay.  The clay will actually be replaced with rubber once the fiberglass shells are finished.  This will be a  three piece mold and allow the head to be hollow so crazy builders can put in a interior ect. Once the clay work is done you paint it with a gloss black spray paint. This acts as a release so the clay falls out of the fiberglass.  They you paint and lay fiberglass on the clay.  Do this three times and presto your mother molds are complete.  Taking turns you remove the clay and then pour rubber into the void and do this three times and presto you no have a fiberglass jacketed three part mold.  More pics later…  

[IMAGE: 281938019.jpg]

[IMAGE: 281938017.jpg]

[IMAGE: 281938014.jpg]

[IMAGE: 281938013.jpg]

All thats left to do is apply the fiberglass and start pouring.. 

Ive finished that portion already will take pictures right before the rubber pour to show you what it looks like.

Both kits are progressing along... look for something in about 2 weeks!  Kits will be boxed up and ready for shippment before PMs are sent out saying they are ready thats why its taking so long trying to get a bunch of each ready before the orders are taken, I hope you understand...

Re: Studio Scale AT-ST under construction!

Ok, heres an update.. ESB AT-ST once the the halves have been clayed up and a twopart fiberglass shell has been made you pour the part 3 core of the mold.

[IMAGE: 282945874.jpg]

After the core has cured you remove one of the outer shells and dig away all the clay.  Make sure you keep it casue that lets you know how much RTV its going to take to pour the half.  In this pic you can see the clay is ready to recieve rubber.

[IMAGE: 282945872.jpg]

This pictures shows the upper jacket. Several holes have to be drilled to allow the RTV to vent as its poured through a hole you cut in the jacket.  Once it vents for about 10 to 15 mins you plug up all the little holes with clay.

[IMAGE: 282945867.jpg]

This pictures shows part 1 and 2 of the RTV has been poured last is the part three!

[IMAGE: 282945894.jpg]

[IMAGE: 282945893.jpg]

Here is the casting!  I took some advise from a member here and minimized the use of clay to fill undercuts.  (Thanks Jason)  I used a runbber with a bit more elongation so it gets down in there really good and replicates the undercuts without the fear of ripping.

[IMAGE: 282945892.jpg]

The casting is sharp, crisp and sturdy.  Even Wall thickness my very frist 3 part mold.  I am happy to report..  it's operational.

Re: Studio Scale AT-ST under construction!

It'll be a little while before I get my ESB built up (some projects in the queue in front of it), but I've been thinking about how to display it.  Specifically, I wonder if the resin legs will tend to bend and sag over time.  Obviously, a freshly-built model will stand very jauntily on its own, but plastics in general, and resins like we use in particular, have this tendency called 'creep' which can cause deformation over time.

My thinking was that I'd either drill a pair of small (~ 1/8") holes in the back and solder a little brass 'fork' to a pole fixed to the display base to actually support all the weight of the model (much like an animation stand, but hopefully less obtrusive); or, to make a support pole that the bottom of the lower body would rest on top of, taking just some of the weight off the legs.  The first option would be more fail-safe, but the second doesn't involve drilling ant non-prototypical holes in the model.  Thoughts?

For anyone who is interested, I found it pretty easy to replace the hoses that come with the kit. Steve did an excellent job casting them, but it is almost impossible to cast them without having the seam line down the hose. And its almost as impossible to remove the seam. An easier solution is to take some thicker gauge solder wire and wrap it with thin gauge aluminum wire (both nice and cheap at the local hardware store). With the solder wire it is nice and flexible and looks great. I'll try to post some pics. I'll also have the exact gauge of the wires used.

Hey guys, 

MT's ROTJ "HERO" AT-ST build is one of a kind!  Want to make sure all props are sent his direction for going the extra step in making a Real Deal construct!  Showing his metal parts made me want to show mine! Well parts that is..

Here's the ROTJ ATST resin kit leg upgrade that I'm working on (got to do something while the resin is cooling!) 

John C the original pattern maker is quite busy with a "few" projects now so I figured I'd lend a razor knife.. hell... I can glue my fingers together with the best of them.

Thanks to all that have pointed out the inaccuracy of the current kit (Size of the legs, feet and top of the neck base detail). Without you we could not make the kit better! :thumbsup  

To correct the small legs that came with the first kit run.. I have used new reference and built correct sized legs. The upper leg is still one main piece with some add on details. the main lower leg will come in three pieces all with steel rod cast into them. These three will be pinned and glued togther to make the one main leg. The lower leg will have steel impregnated in it as well. 

Im finishing all the rivet work now and will start on the bigger feet... you know what they say about AT-ST's with big feet.... more accurate I think MT's build proves that. 

So here is a break down of the process to upgrade the current resin kit. 

1. Secure more accurate reference ... DONE!

2. Compare the parts for scale and detail... DONE!

3. Draw new designs for parts by breaking the parts down as they were originaly made. DONE 

I first did these on paper then I did drawings in AutoCad and IllistratorCS2 and the parts can be cut in any type of material. Problem is to do it in alluminum.. it costs almost twice the cost of the entire ROTJ Kit just to have leg parts cut. Thats not counting the drill and tap work that needs to be added to the overall price.

4. Have the parts cut. I chose Acrylic and Styrene as these will be mold masters for the new kit run set for January 09.

[IMAGE: 343967678.jpg]

[IMAGE: 343967517.jpg]

some parts are missing as they were used on the master patterns. Nice to punch parts out that are perfect, little to no sanding..

5. assemble the parts... these pics show raw and assembled parts together... the last pic is the size comparison to the OLD set of legs currenly issuded with the kit.

[IMAGE: 343965293.jpg]

[IMAGE: 343965262.jpg]

[IMAGE: 343963277.jpg]

[IMAGE: 343965262.jpg]

[IMAGE: 343965349.jpg]

you can really tell the difference on the size. Will make the new kit pop when assembled! I'll keep the thread updated when these puppies are ready and the new ROTJ Studio Scale kit is ready. A fellow board member will be getting the first set and will add them to his already built ATST! Thanks for looking.

Answer. So a free standing ATST (ROTJ) will be inaccurate.. it cannot stand by itself.
