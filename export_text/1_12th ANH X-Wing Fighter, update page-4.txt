Well all this talk lately about the large x-wing miniature got me fired up again to plod on with some work i had already done on this baby!. I feel bad cause i should have finished the tie-bomber like months ago but i got distracted once again by this sucker lol!. Anyway bomber first to finish then back to this. This x-wing will not feature the same detail as the ILM version, i plan to copy the 1/24th version details scaled up by 2x. I prefer the 1/24th details rather than the bashed 1/12th version.

Parts in photos are still rough especially the large copied saturn cover and need to be trimmed and detailed.

Armature will be machined aluminium with styrene fuselage built over a wooden former then removed and detailed.

Good to hear opinions from you guys on this baby.

Grabs in 1 second (no second chances) dries instantly so you can carry on working in less than a minute. The corrugated sheet that is wrapped over the saturn covers used this glue without clamps, just a masking tape guide line and voila!. Just an FYI for thoughts on producing a 1/12 scale kit of this, the 1/24 kit was about 5lbs of resin, and if i remember right (I googled to check this) something 2x the size will take 8x the volume to make.  Since the V3 was already largely hollow this could be potentially 8x the resin if done in full kit form.  I'm sure that a fiberglass body would be much lighter if you someone choose to do something like that.... but the 8x volume is something to think about when it comes to what a kit would cost. I don't want to discourage anyone, just wanted to share what i found out.....

I had to think about this because i have been working on a 1/12 scale pattern of another ship myself.....

Looks like you are off to a great start!

I'm fortunate to have full access to a full model engineering workshop (my Dads) 2 lathes, computer mill etc... .Been looking at various ways to build this with my main concern in that the wings will be fairly weighty so i forsee metal support needed throughout the wing structure. Re: 4 Footer X-Wing  1/12th scale

That it is....found items can be real gems.I never machined acrylic.I watched others do it "in class" with a very small CNC mill(about the size of a hobby top drill press).They machined it dry,with water,or a water/dishsoap soloution for lubrcating/cooling effects.The finish was well....it varried.I think the rpm's might have either been too slow or too high as well as the feed rates.The rake angles of the milling cutters may have been too aggressive also?The finish was well..too slow it was rough or even sort of balled up/melted as it machined(most noteable when machined dry).One piece cracked(they were maching circles/pockets.Some of this may have been due to the type of acrylic used.I think "cast acrylic" probably machines the best where as extruded arcylic being the cheap stuff doesn't take to it as well.I think the mills probably weren't cut out for it either,the rpm's were probably the most detrimental.A router can do somwhere between 15,000-40,000 RPM's and I think those small lathes maxed out around 3,000 maybe up to 5,000 rpm's?The milling cutters wer somewhere between 1/4 inch to as small as .060-.030(very small).I forget,that was a long time ago.

Well, here's a little progress Iv'e made on this bird!.

All the wing skins are cut out of styrene with the one in the pics having the cutouts made and scribing done on the inner wing. The braces in between are to show the heights needed. Just the inner cutouts to cleanup!.

The whole strip will be molded and cast in one piece so I'm trying not to make any nasty undercuts which should'nt be an issue here!.

The green part is from an Italeri 1/35th Elefant to match the Fujimi part which is just the right size, just needs a bit of modification and will be molded and cast for the number required.

The other parts from other kits are to show size comparison, the kits being a 1/24th SS X-wing, MPC and a Finemolds version.

This is a big model,so you'll have to think big parts. The original ILM guys used 1/35th scale tank models,and 1/8th scale truck,and motorcycle kits. Most of these were Airfix,Hasagawa,and Tamiya kits,as they were very popular with modelers back in the late seventies,and early eighties.

The "Captain Crapboard" models were awful - I know I have one of these kits,and it's horrible! This isn't a stab at Scott,or those that have one of them. At least he took the initiative to research the models that were involved in the kitbashing that took place then. It's just that these could've been a lot better! There are quite a few inconsistancies that could've been addressed,but when you're the only one in town offering something that no one else has,it's hard for people to say no! 

I'm sure that I'll recieve a lot of flak for this post,but this IS the reason that Mike Salzo made the effort to make it more accurate to the original ILM models. I would offer a more refined version with a correctly shaped,and angled fuselage,and R2-D2 unit,but I would be called a recaster,unless I could put tells in this to show that it's mine that would be obvious in photos on the internet! What would be the point then,as it would no longer be accurate? I'd also include extra parts that aren't available on any garage kit(which I won't mention here),including an armature for a reasonable price. This is something that think I would only sell to people that I personally know,and trust. I'd also have to make mine bigger,so that it wouldn't be confused with other kits,or being accused of recasting another manufacturers' model.

All this is the reason I decided that a Four foot Return of the Jedi model would be more lucrative,and realistic in terms of not being called a recaster on any forum! 

I know that this is a monster to handle,but if I can do it - you can do it too! I'm a former machinist,as the mutual friend of me,and my best friend of twenty plus years decided to take off with all the tools that would make this job a lot easier! Needless to say,we had to go out of business,as we no longer had the tools to make the parts we needed to stay afloat. He left owing over $10,000.00 in debt,and we made most of the money that the business did make! This is a major setback,as now we both have to figure out how we're going to make money now. With what little tooling,and money I have left,I'm still able to make this. It'll just take me a lot longer to do it now!  

If you have the will - there is a way. You can do it Stu!

Hi guys, here's some progress, namely on the cannons and wing panels!.

The pics show some of the multitude of parts needed for the cannons in machined aluminium, plastruct tube and brass.

One thing with Plastruct tube extrusions is the problem of them not being true round!. Very hard to turn indeed, so wooden bucks have to be made, fitted and then turned. This brings to mind the problem with the Tie Bomber, being the Airfix Saturn parts for the rear engines not fitting to the Plastruct tubes perfectly. IMO the cut down Airfix parts are more true than said Plastruct items!.

Found a better kit for the Sherman rear part being the Dragon kit with the right suspension parts to boot, matches the 1/72nd Hasegawa one better than the 1/35th Tamiya version. I know about the older Tamiya release but the Dragon kit is far more crisp and detailed. Iv'e used the Tamiya turret bottom on the Dragon hull as its the better shape, modified to fit and be removeable.

Still lots to do on the cannons such as adding the paneling on the main pods, make the heat sink part (this will be machined either in acrylic or alloy) and a few other details, but they're getting there and I'm very happy with the result not to mention the size!. 

All wings have been cut out and are in various stages of assembly. The wings in the pics have some panel details added (I just could'nt wait).

Seems I'm in the shop every weekend now, fabricating parts but hey I'm loving it!.

Ok you X-Wing buffs, I would appreciate opinions on this 1/12th nose cone in it's raw form Iv'e been slaving over for a while now!.

The contours are taken from photo ref with many templates being taken and used and adjusted as the sculpting goes along. The old eyeball goes along way too lol!.

The material here is good old laminated balsa, glued in rough blocks and then taken down to the rough shape in 2 halves.

The cone as it is now needs to be taken down 1mm all round to allow for the sheet styrene final surface. When I'm happy with the final shape the 2 halves will then be coated in a special PVA (one coat brushed on, this goes rough so needs a light sand, second coat brushed on and dries like smooth glass, incredible really!) and then the styrene is applied over this in strips/sheets to fit the contours using fast CA.

The styrene will then be sanded and this is when all the edges will be more rounded, the wood core is left with fairly straight edges before the styrene surface.

The top of the cone will have an insert for the trench detail panel and the cutout for this will happen when I'm 100% happy with the contours overall.

Cheers chaps

Stu

It's coming, but I'd say the upper slope looks a little short and steep. Certainly compared to the 1/24 models. Can't comment on the big one as I've not got a decent profile of the nose to hand. 

One thing to bear in mind is that in terms of form, the nose and the rest of the fuselage are a single entity, and every line has to be related to, and harmonized with, every other line from tip to well past the cockpit. The relationship of the upper slope of the nose to the upper slope of the fuselage for instance is absolutely crucial, and extremely subtle. I wouldn't want to model either in isolation from the other. The  V3 nose for instance slopes too early but this error can only be seen when the nose is seen in context with the fuselage. Independent of the fuse, the error is virtually undetectable.

Anyway, amazing build. Good luck with it!

The beauty of having it virtually, in the computer is that you can make it any scale you want...if your willing to spend the money to bring it into the analog world.

Having said that .. .when  I first started this big-X back in 2008 ( had to put it on hold, -sorry).

Since that time, things have been moving so rapidly with..wait for it ...rapid -prototyping. :$  -mainly in the area of stereolithograpy (aka 3d-Printing)

This is nice for me as I can keep the panel lines intact when printed. In the cace of CNC milling it works out best - to scribe the panel lines by had after it has been milled.  regarding the panel lines the will be the same with and dept as the 1/24 scale (aprox: .006 " if memory serves)

It works out bets to keep the panel lines thin; otherwise  the miniature would start to look like a big toy.
