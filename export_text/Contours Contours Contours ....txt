There are two ways I would do it. One way is to take absolutely precise measurements either off a drawing or model and recreate those measurements, minus the skin, then to transfer those measurements to plastic or other medium precisely, in order to get the correct curvatures.

Another way is to create a model in a 3D modelling package then to slice sections in an orderly pattern to create the sections. Lay strip plastic over top and sand to a nice curvature.

I guess a third way to do it is to carve a buck out of balsa or soft wood and sand it to shape. Then vacuform stryrene over it. This method, can be less accurate if you don't have proper contour measuring tools.

I'm looking forward to other ideas that members would offer.

I agree, I've used 3D modeling programs to design builds that were all done by hand. No 3D printing or laser cutting, just printed out the ribs on paper and cut them with scissors. I've also designed (well coppied a design really) a space ship from a magazine by drawing it all up by hand. I used the side profile for the top registration of the ribs and the top profile for the side registration of the ribs. I then freehanded the curve, cut them all out of card stock and stacked them all together to see the flow and which ones needed to be adjusted. After I was happy with that I transfered the shape to acrylic and cut out the ribs.

To get it as accurate as possible, you really need the top view, bottom view, side view, front view, and rear view. I wrote one of the only books on the subject of making drawings for something like this. You can get it here:

www.ModelersNotebook.com

I agree, a 3D model is the way to go. If you can find one that's accurate enough, it will serve as a three-dimensional template to help you figure out the correct cross section at each location.
