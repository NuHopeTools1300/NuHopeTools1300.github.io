Seems to be a few TOS Vipers lately, so thought I would add mine to the mix.

I haven't built a model of any kind since I was....oh..10 maybe, so let's see what kind of damage i can do to this poor innocent viper.

I was fortunate to get one of JM/Jestefareans Viper long OOP kit. I am tickled pink to have this particular version of the TOS Viper becuase it is a casting descendant of the Icons offering, which is a descendant of a pyro viper (as per various postings on the rpf). Which i do find an interesting statement...as I also see written that there were no pyro viper models, as the explosions were apparently all optical trickery. hmmm.

Because of generational shrinkage this offering is of course now a bit smaller than hero vipers. There is/was a similar kit from MiM but is considered to be inferior quality wise, so am very happy to have obtained this. Also now available is the Mr. Salzo created , correctly scaled new build Viper, available now from Jase72 on SSM. 

Any...here we go. 

Had to fix the seams on the right side of the body. This seems to be common to this kit. Easily fixed with 10 mins of fine snding, some putty and a few more minutes of sanding next day. But aside from that...there is next to nothing to do...just a bit of the usual light flashing.

I put in the brass rods (between the two fuselage halves for support)...glued, and gave the body a coat of primer. For first go i will not be lighting.

I just wanted to finally see something with some paint (even just primer) after pestering folks on here, and slowly gathering an inventory of kits to build over the last year or so.

Well, here is my extremely noob answer, so please, I stand to be correct as needed...but.....

In this particular case, the black has little use for light blocking. It basically serves 3 purposes.

First is to blacken all the small nooks and crannies and corners, so when you go over with the lighter surface colours, shading and such is visible in said crannies.

Second...and it seems the technique is in evidence on the viper models, but for sure it was used in the star wars models...and many of the BSG modelers were alumni of ILM. After you cover the black with your lighter main colour, you take said lighter colour down using steel wool or such. Thus wearing the lighter colour off unevenly, giving an  uneven wear to the paint job, giving the model that nice used universe look. Not clean and even. Also, if doing some actual paint chipping...you will see a somewhat more realistic black underneath...not resin. 

Of course, both goals of the above (shading and wear) can be achieved using other painting methods (and none are right or wrong), but this is how it was done at that point in time for these studio models.

And the third...is well...that's how they did it.
