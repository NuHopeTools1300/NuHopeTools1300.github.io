Sorry Dan, I beg to differ. I collect shuttle model kits and have done so for many years. So I can spot the differences between the toolings at a glance rather easily. I have examples of BOTH the Airfix and Entex kits (actually, Minicraft in my case, but it is the same G-Mark tooling as I have verified that by comparing it with an Entex kit) and they are different molds all together from one another.

Entex indeed distributed other kits, but from the far east, NOT Airfix stuff to my knowledge (if so, in rather small numbers and more than likely the same kits that Airfix imported into the UK as opposed to their own molds). So the Entex shuttle mold was from a Japanese company called G-Mark and it was not related to the Airfix tooling at all. Airfix was available in the USA at the time from Airfix (in somewhat small numbers, but they could be found if one looked), USAirfix (until about 1979-80) and MPC from 81 onwards until Airfix got a better US distributorship sometime in the late 1980s.

You may want to think about adding some PU foam to the inside, but you need to be really careful with the heat.  I've used smooth on foam-it 8 and it is much, much easier to control than a lot of the other foams.  The key would be to mix a very small amount and brush it onto the inside of the hull, a few very thin layers until you got where you wanted. 

The good part is that if you do use the foam you can have something to glue threaded nuts or hollow rods in strategic  positions to have something to mount the back plate details to.  You may even be able to carve a skirt to mount the back stuff to.  

The key will be to control the heat so you don't warp the pull.  I've used this stuff and I think it generates much less heat than some of the other stuff.  But go slow, very, very thin layers at first.

mike

The key would be to mix a very small amount and brush it onto the inside of the hull, a few very thin layers until you got where you wanted. 

The good part is that if you do use the foam you can have something to glue threaded nuts or hollow rods in strategic  positions to have something to mount the back plate details to.  You may even be able to carve a skirt to mount the back stuff to.  

The key will be to control the heat so you don't warp the pull.  I've used this stuff and I think it generates much less heat than some of the other stuff.  But go slow, very, very thin layers at first.

mike

As far as the assumption that the parts won't fit under the skirt...("as we've all been noticing" - just who exactly are 'we all', anyway?)...there's a bit of irony here that I don't think  anyone but me is actually in a position to appreciate: 

I'm the only one who has  produced patterns for the radar dish and the  engines.  They are based  entirely on my own calcs / dims.  Yet,  everyone seems to accept the dims  of those parts as gospel, when they  were calculated by the same idiot  (me) who decided how long the skirt  should be, which everyone seems to accept is FUBAR'd. Let's not forget the length of the skirt dictated the length of the upper hull/turret...and, oh yeah, all the original kit parts fit just fine on that.

In my view the problem with the FG S1 pattern has more to do with the   fact that it's a scaled up version of the FM hull, which exhibits an   incorrect placement of the wingwell openings.   I am not comfortable  saying that the entire skirt is too short.  It may very well be, but  I've not demonstrated that to be true, and I've taken this model farther  than anyone.

For all anyone here  knows, because none of you has actually built a mockup with all of the parts inside the  skirt, everything will fit just fine if the wingwells are moved down. 

I'm just pointing out that it's not prudent to draw conclusions from unproven assumptions.

And to be clear, the reason I sold the FG hull and parts set wasn't because I believed it wasn't buildable.  I sold it because I knew that I would never be offered the opportunity to properly ID the rest of the model - the cockpit interior. Dan and I had both been working on that, based on the limited ref we had, and we had done remarkably well.  But, we had exhausted all of the reference we had and no amount of gamma correction was going to reveal anything more.  And I had come too far to accept building what would be an interpretation of the cockpit.  

Lastly, if the skirt on the FG model is too short, it's also something that can be fixed.  Get a Dremel, cut it off, fill the inside of the skirt, adjust the size with some Apoxie sculpt and sweat equity, and vac over it.

Hey guys,

I've been working on the Slave One for some time now, but I just resolved the length issue yesterday.

It seems that FM used the ILM pics for their Slave One (the ones in the Chronicles). At the first glance the side view looks great but actualy there's a lot of lense distorsion and the nose (front skirt and cockpit) are not in it's integrality, not only because of the lense distorsion but also because the cam is slighty off center.

After a lot of calculs, I was able to get a side view which matches the underside view.

The result is a skirt that is 604 mm long, 336 mm wide.

From the very front of the skirt to the wing gap it's about 198 mm, the wing gap is about 70,5 mm and from the gap to the end of the skirt it's about 335,5 mm.

All the parts, including the biggest (Entex Porsche, radar dish etc) fit perfectly and the wing wells are at the right place.

Hope that helps !
