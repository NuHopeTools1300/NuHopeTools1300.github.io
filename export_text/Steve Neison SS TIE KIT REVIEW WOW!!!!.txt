It's not so much rocket science, I just don't think it's feasible for a company to easily reproduce it. I wish someone would, as it hasn't been manufactured like this for decades. The story is that they eventually realized (by the early 80's) that they could save on manufacturing costs and not affect the performance by spacing out the louvers/slats and making the set angle of the slats more extreme, hence the contemporary product.

The louvers are actually comprised of a bronze wire that's about 1/8 of a millimeter wide that is woven around the slats and holds them at a precise angle. It's more of a tolerance thing that's the most impressive aspect about it. I can't get my camera in macro to really achieve a proper focus, but here's what I ended up with:

[IMAGE: 2053568.jpeg]

[IMAGE: 2108955.jpeg]

Try to scale the images down so the pennies match the diameter of one held up to the screen. The louver wire is pretty hard to make out with the naked eye--just can't imagine replicating the weaving process at such a microscopic level would be a walk in the park.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Thanks for posting the images. 

That tells me a lot. It looks like they are made by a process of weaving and sewing. 

The metal slats are feed through at the correct angle. At regular intervals the equivalent of a sewing needle takes two wires and wraps them to make a stitch. This whole process takes place for the entire length of the shade at once. 

But the stitch process could make the correct angle, thus simplifying the job even more. 

Making a machine to do the job isn't as costly as would be assumed. For Ties you don't need a machine that big. The ideal thing would be to find a Kool Shade machine and reverse engineer it, or cut it down to meet your needs. 

The toughest part is the needles, and how much force is required. 

Industrial machines are really very simple, they only look complex because they are series of steps.
