Dat, 

As to which are the most accurate models... I'll let others get into that. You will find good information on kit parts used to build the original X-Wings in some of the threads listed in the One Stop X-Wing sticky thread. You'll need to do a bit of digging but the info is there.

As to your question about resin vs. 3D printed molds. You've phrased the questions wrong, it should be resin vs. 3D printed "parts". The original Star Wars models were detailed using parts from plastic model kits. Mostly tanks and other military models but a far number of ship, cars, trucks and plane kits were used as well (and let's not forget SeaLab!) Although there is some evidence that recasts of parts may have been used (the droid strip from the X-wing for example was cast and used on several other models like the Laser Tower and Death Star Surface tiles) for the most part is appears that they just used up tons of kits. they were relatively inexpensive and readily available, unlike today.

The absolute best type of part to use on the recreation of a studio scale model is a part from the correct model kit originally used. A so-called donor part. Some of these kits are still commercially available, though many are no longer manufactured and have to be found on Ebay or at model kit shows.

However, since a lot of these kits are rare and expensive, people have taken to molding the original parts and casting multiple duplicates in polyurethane casting resin. If the molds are well made and the castings carefully poured (often in a pressure tank to eliminate air bubbles), once painted,  the castings can be indistinguishable from the originals donor parts. The only real disadvantage is that you have to use super glue or epoxy to glue down the parts rather than solvent cement.

If donor kit parts are not available, or the kit from which they came cannot be identified then the part can be modeled in a computer using photo reference and some dimensional assumptions and 3D printed. The quality of these re-created parts depends on several things, the quality of the reference photos, the 3d modeling skills of the person doing the work and the 3D printer being used to make the parts. All 3d printed parts have layering artifacts, in high end SLA type machines these can be virtually invisible to the eye, on FDM type machines they can be pretty obvious.

Depending on the cost, size and quantity needed of the 3D printed parts sometimes the printed part is used on a model, sometime the printed part is molded and cast in resin. The latter is a better solution for long term part life.

Hope this helps.

In addition to what Dave said, don't worry if you're using kitparts castings or a resin model kit. Several of the original screen used models were resin (X-Wings, Y-Wing, TIE Fighter/Interceptor, Snowspeeder, AT-AT, ROTJ AT-ST etc to name a few 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]
) and a lot of models used resin parts, like Dave said, the X-Wing droid strip, but also several plant-ons made for the 5' Millenium Falcon were also used on the Y-Wing, 32" Falcon, ESB Star Destroyer, Laser Tower etc) same with AT-AT parts used on the ESB Star Destroyer or 32" Falcon. 

For years we went for "all plastic" scratched models getting in mind it would be a true reproduction of the screen used models, which is wrong for a lot of models, most of the time if you want to be accurate, go for resin ! 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]
 Mainly for the TIE and X-Wing you mentionned, for the Falcon that's another story...

Regarding a screen accurate Falcon, nothing available regarding the 5', you're on your own if you want to scratch it (there's a good thread here with partmaps,blueprints using photogrammetry etc), for the 32" (ESB) you have the Master Replica which is nice (even if all the kitparts have been reproduced instead of using real kitparts but they are very well done) or the De Agostini Falcon, less accurate in term of kitparts but the overall shape is very good.

Another point I'd like to make is that many times we get too wrapped up in using just the right perfect accurate kit part, or casting there of. Keep in mind that any replica made of a Studio Scale model is going to be an artistic interpretation. I have a great deal of respect and admiration for the models built using all the correct kit parts, but at the same time, there is nothing wrong with "winging it" a bit. I liken it to trying to be faithful to the "creative intent" of the original, while not getting bogged down trying to find all the right parts. I do a little of everything on my projects. I just purchased the correct Jagd Panther to use as a donor kit for some parts on my studio scale Laser Tower, while at the same time I am fine that some of the other parts are scratch built out of styrene while others I've computer modeled and 3D printed and still others are completely made up. As long as it looks good. I'm okay with it. And I would rather have a model that is done than one sitting on the shelf unfinished while I track down some obscure rare model kit. Besides, 99.99(999)% percent of the people that ever see the model are not going to know, or care, if a particular part is correct. The important thing is to have fun recreating these iconic models in whatever way makes you happy (except recasting someone else's resin kit, but that's a different topic!).

I don't know if the De Agostini have been tooled from the same files as the MR Falcon, however for having seen the 3D scan of the Falcon used by MR, I can tell you that they use it only for the basic shape and maybe the plating but not to reproduce the kit parts ! The original kitparts have been used to recreate the details on the MR by the Chinese manufacturers, while the original attempt was to use them on the master.

So De Agostini maybe used the same files to create the basic shape but they're on their own to recreate the details (which are far from being accurate) and will never be as good as the MR was because the MR was resin, that means undercuts are allowed and a lot of kitparts are separate injection molded parts, unlike the De Agostini. 

I don't need to see the whole De Agostini Falcon to say it is less accurate in term of details than the MR (which is not as accurate as a Falcon made with original parts
