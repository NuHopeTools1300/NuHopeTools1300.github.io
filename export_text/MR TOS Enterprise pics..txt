This is the First Engineering Prototype (FEP), many things have already been addressed.

The MR TOS Enterpise is going to be injection molded in a strong ABS material similar to the AT-AT legs. It's a big mold, but the folks on this project were determined that the sagging that plagued previous versions of this famous starship would NOT be an issue this time.

All the screws will be invisible on the finished pieces.

The aim with this piece is to provide collectors with the definitive small version TOS Enterrpise.

Fingers crossed.

Barry

The "burn marks " coming back from the leading edge of the primary hull can be seen as well.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The streaks on the front have already been toned down, they're very light. And the darker gray details have been lightened.

As for the green overspray that covered the Enterprise model, it is believed this was added to help prevent blue spill during matte photography. It was not supposed to be part of the E's colour scheme but was a solution to help prevent holes appearing in the mattes when blue light would pool and reflect back off the light gray of the model.

Therein lies the rub with the E. Do you paint her how she looked in the flesh or do you paint her how she "appears" on screen? Her colouration is all over the place shot to shot in the show.

It's a tough question, which the fans have debated for decades.

Personally I think a model should look "how the miniature really looked"... but adjusted for scale. So for example the weathering on a half scale (5.5 foot) model would be more apparent than on this small quarter-scale model.

It's a model-of-the-miniature, so to speak.

This as opposed to "how the ship looked on TV", which is a pandora's box. As Barry pointed out, it looks different from shot to shot. Plus there are color timing errors all over the place, people's memories, opinions, all sorts of crap to contend with.

At least the miniature actually existed. :lol

... don't try to read too much into these. One image is of an 11-foot model shot with a studio camera setup. The other is a 33" model shot with a modern digital camera. The lenses, perspective, and scale is totally different.

When a company puts together its product list there are always worries as to whether there will be enough customers wanting the products to justify the expense of developing and bringing them to market. Even with an established company like Master Replicas it's still a lot like making a movie. Will it be a blockbuster, a modest hit, make it's money back, be a slow burner, or a flop? It's a crap shoot.

The level of interest in the Enterprise and the Millennium Falcon threads here and on other boards is encouraging and will hopefully translate those releases into blockbusters 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]

Both projects are complicated, costly and risky for many different reasons. The Falcon is large, complex and hugely detailed. The Enterprise is large, complex and deceptively simple. MR took its customary approach of quality first with these pieces. The Falcon is a beautifully engineered, solid, faithful and long lasting replica, as were the AT-AT, the Snowspeeder and the Y-wing before it. The research and detail are simply astounding...

The Enterprise, while looking simpler, is equally difficult. Its beautiful and distinctive shape requires structural support. So many fans had bad experiences with their Unobtanium E's, MR determined that history would not repeat itself. The approach taken is wholly different and vastly more expensive to pull off.

Hopefully the investment will be worth it, but really it's all down to that roll of the dice. There are no guarantees when dealing with the public's taste. Will there be a market for two of the most famous spacecraft in all of science fiction? Fingers crossed the answer is yes.

Barry
