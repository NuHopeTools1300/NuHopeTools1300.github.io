The budget required to attempt a serious studio scale build is simply beyond my means.  I do have a habit of watching Ebay for sales of boxes of built up models, often broken, or in parts.  Over the course of time, I have run across some amazing bargains by doing this.  It’s fun, even though it often entails much time from me to fix someone’s glue bombs.  For this particular build, the crucial portions of an AMT Space: 1999 Eagle Transporter virtually fell into my hands, so I determined to give it a try.  

I had just seen a clear image of this ship appeared – thank you, Odist!  Making a print out of that image, enlarged to match the known size of my Eagle Transporter pod was next.  

I wanted variations on the word Eagle, since the really recognizable bits of this starship were from an Eagle model kit.  A quick Google later, I discovered Eagle in Italian is Aquila.  With that detail squared away, gathering up suitable parts from various parts boxes followed.

Starting at the nose, I needed the tip of a Matra missile pod from a 1/24 scale Airfix Harrier.  Rob in Scotland graciously responded to a posting of mine about this part, and donated one he wasn’t using thank you!  Next in line, unidentified parts which I faked from some dental brush travel containers.  Then we start with the Eagle parts, namely, the forward and aft grid work connecting sections.  These were glued together and then attached to the next unidentified section.  It was simulated from sheet styrene, and that was attached to the Eagle pod.  Three of the top portions from the Eagle landing pod were then attached to the roof of the pod.  

The back section of the Aquila should be made from two side by side sections of Hasegawa’s 1/72 Leopold Railgun, which I don’t have.  I therefore used 1mm acrylic sheet to construct that part, and then added detailing strips which have oval detailing holes punched.  Yes, the holes were individually punched, yes, I am obviously nuts.  The Railgun kit has an ammunition box and one each needs to be placed on the side aft ends of the structure.  Mobieus’s Chariot had some parts that looked good, and with additions of sheet acrylic and styrene detailing appear to be very close to the right size.

Plastruct styrene sheet, 0.40 thick makes up the platform the entire collection rests on, and panel lines were scribed before any parts were glued down.  Sections were cut to allow for the correct height spacing of the Eagle kit parts along the center of the styrene sheet.  Epoxy putty, placed in the underside of an old aircraft drop tank became a substitute for the Monogram B-29 A-bomb part, and two pieces made for the port and starboard sides.

That’s where I am at for the moment – I’ll post more when I get more done!
