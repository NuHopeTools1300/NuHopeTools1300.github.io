Hello everyone, since this is such a great forum for scratch building I thought I would share my latest project with you. I started this SS Moon Bus back in January but it has been in development for over two years. I began by making molds and casting copies of the many kit pieces that detail the model. To date there are over twenty molds and I still have a few more to make. I have all but just a handful of the original parts but that handful still needs to be identified. A few weeks ago I started the landing gear. I glued up some MDF for the three major components, the three pontoon feet, the plate above those and the ribbed support block on top. I sanded the blanks to thickness, cut them to rough size, then started machining them. I would go has far as I could with one part then realize I needed another part for the next set up so I would work on that part. I've already had to remake the block. My original idea was to mold up a machined blank but that wasn't working out well so now I'm going to cast some urethane blanks and machine those individually. You can see the first blank in the background of some of the pictures. The plan now is to finish the plate and make one outside pontoon and one center pontoon and then make molds of all three. Thanks for looking, I'll update as work progresses

Joe

Here are some shots of two “kit” pieces that I had to make. The first series is how I made a mirror image of that familiar Atomic cannon part that's on the side of the bus. I wasn't happy with the idea of just flipping it over for use on the other side so I cut apart a couple of castings and reassembled them. Then I used that part as a master for a new mold. The second series is the construction of one of the parts that could not be sourced. I remember reading once that someone thought it might be half of a gas pump but I don't know from which kit. I don't think Martin Bower sourced this part either when he made his model. Anyway, this is what I came up with.

I'm pretty much done with the machining of the pontoons, now it's just fine tuning with hand tools, several sand, prime cycles Those bevel cuts were a real hassle even though I've used similar set ups for years making furniture. There were a few miss cuts and a couple of classic Joe blunders, but nothing so terrible that I couldn't salvage my blanks. I've gotten real good at scabbing on more material and recutting. I don't like doing that and usually have enough test pieces on hand. I initially had plenty of blanks for the pontoons, but when it came down to the final cuts, all I used was one test piece and my two good blanks. It worked out in the end  though, which is all that matters.

This may not look like a lot of progress since my last update but I've been spending a lot of time working on the construction drawings for the hull, job hunting and getting my metal lathe up and running. After getting the lathe two years ago and not doing anything with it I finally got better tool posts for it and ground some cutters and started turning. The first part I made was the ring flange that goes between the plate and the center column boot and the second was the boot itself. I haven't used this type of lathe since 1987 and was never much of a machinist anyway, but I'm quite happy with the results. The boot will be cast in black “rubber” urethane. I also made some brass bushings to fit into the six outer holes for the smaller pistons. Originally, I tried drilling a counter bore followed by a through hole but they weren't concentric so I came up with this idea instead. It worked out really well. I'm also getting the two pontoon blanks ready to be molded up. That's all for now, thanks for looking.

Joe
