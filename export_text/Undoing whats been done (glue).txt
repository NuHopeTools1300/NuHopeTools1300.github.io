Depending on when it was assembled, it could have been glued with a solvent cement. If that's the case you could be limited on splitting the seams apart depending on how much solvent was used. You could place the glued parts into the freezer and in an hour or two remove them and gently[ pry the parts apart with a hobby knife. Be careful as the plastic will also become brittle and could fracture.

I've had some good luck using a 50:50 to 25:75 mix of water and simple green. Throw the parts into a pot with the burner on low, and let it sit for a while with the top off. There's no telling how long it could take, some people say up to 24 hours but I'm not sure that's necessary. Best to try it with some parts you don't care about first and see if it gets you anywhere. After 12-24 hours or so, you'll probably be able to ply apart the parts at the glue joints. 

Someone else recommended using brake fluid as well, but I never tried that.

Polly S makes a paint removal product called Easy Lift Off.  You spread it over the painted surface, let it sit overnight, and the paint can, in most instances, be wiped off with a damp rag.  for tight corners, you might need to dip a toothbrush in the stuff and scrub it off.

I have heard about brake fluid and oven cleaner, but since I found this stuff, I haven't felt the need to experiment with anything else.

As for the glue - odds are some sort of solvent glue was used.  Liquid glues were available back then (and in some cases, were more 'potent' than the stuff we use today).  The old Testor's tube glue that we grew up with (and the Type 'S' Cement from Revell), are just a variation on that same solvent - just with a thickening agent.  If only a little was used, and it was the tube type, you should be able to break it apart using brute force.  A more even, solid bond will require you to cut it apart.

Use the Polly S Easy Lift Off Stuff.  It works great, even on the super thick, hi-gloss enamels of yesteryear.

Gene

I have been told that caustic soda is good for paint removal and glue breakdown (old tube stuff). I haven't tried it myself, as I'm not sure of the mix required (i.e. how much water) and it's is a bit nasty. You are supposed to add acid to the caustic solution before disposing of it. 

BTW, adding oil (olive, vegetable, etc) to caustic soda makes soap, so if you get it on your skin, it uses the oils in your skin and buggers you up a bit. If you wear rubber gloves and eye-protection and take the necessary precautions you should use with any chemical it should be ok.

As I said, I haven't tried this personally, but a couple of people have told me that this works and they have tried it. You may need to leave the kit soaking for a half-day to a day for all the glue to become soft though.

Cheers and take care, JT
