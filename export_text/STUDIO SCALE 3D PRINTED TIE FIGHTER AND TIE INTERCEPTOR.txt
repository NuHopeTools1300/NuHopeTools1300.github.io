These metal parts are the display stand for the model……

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/4c3e5962-9ce0-427f-b61a-ab9f4d119b91-jpeg.1671622/]

The parts fit together like so-

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/ce302cf1-51f4-43b2-8a7f-0bdf441a7a44-jpeg.1671626/]

The horizontal metal rod gets inserted through the Fuselage. The ends of the metal rod each have a threaded hole so you can attach the wings of the Tie Fighter with screws. This is a nice bonus in case I ever have to disassemble the model, all I have to do is remove the screws.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/0faf0810-ccce-402e-bc0d-1da7d261238b-jpeg.1671628/]

The model then sits on top of the display stand like so-

The bottom of the stand can be screwed to any material I like. Most likely I will use a piece of painted MDF.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20b0eea3-4690-4dcb-90bd-d8784f377c0d-jpeg.1671633/]

Before attaching the wings I had to widen the hole to accept the metal rod so when I disassemble the model for painting everything comes apart easily.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/2318599d-6c4d-4d94-9bea-e35923961b08-jpeg.1671636/]

Then the wings get screwed on with the provided screws and washers.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/04149269-e851-4d30-8088-901ec0d04a6c-jpeg.1671637/]

I sanded down the sides of the details which cover up the screws so I can disassemble the model as needed. These parts fit together so perfectly and the tolerances are so tight that if I didn’t sand the parts, once installed they would never come apart.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/92485e63-98ac-49e7-adbd-93cfad1f132c-jpeg.1671643/]

Done! 30 minutes of work. With the exception of the wings, everything is held together with a friction fit and nothing is glued.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/9be88f34-ab55-4768-9307-9ac6beb8d88d-jpeg.1671644/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/c336a0aa-6b7a-4868-8cd8-4d73b2bb5812-jpeg.1671645/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/42c80969-5b5b-4464-942f-d59ac92a373e-jpeg.1671646/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/4554ac00-52c3-4e77-99a7-c51d74e1478d-jpeg.1671649/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/44ba32b9-608a-4511-8fa7-e8576728f9d4-jpeg.1671650/]

I have a quick update to share. It is my understanding that there are off the shelf commercially available paints that are a close color match for the Tie Fighter. With that in mind I went to my local Home Depot and purchased a selection of gray paint and primers to test.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/234d6f72-74c9-47e7-8c55-7f4c06dc83a2-jpeg.1694009/]

The Rust-Oleum Enamel Satin Slate Blue looks like a good match so I decanted the paint from the spray can which yielded approximately 1/4 pint of paint.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/46e5a78a-cfe2-4f47-8039-c1893bc715d8-jpeg.1694010/]

The paint has a fairly thick consistency so I thinned the paint with Testors Enamel Thinner at a ratio of 50% paint to 50% thinner  so I could shoot it with my airbrush.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/15526b49-9559-4426-b1af-9cc9e2936d2c-jpeg.1694025/]

I’m getting very good results with the Rust-Oleum enamel paint. It’s a paint and primer all in one so it has excellent adhesion properties and it is very durable. I can sand it, buff it, polish it and use solvent based fillers on it and the paint won’t wrinkle or melt. The only downside is it takes a few days to dry, but that is par for the course when working with enamel paint.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/8da25090-46f9-4b09-8a40-1e5f9bd437ae-jpeg.1703165/]

That parts for the Tie Fighter are all painted with the Rust-Oleum Satin Slate Blue.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/9e383545-998b-44a4-9213-542ca2405f61-jpeg.1703166/]

Up next I’m going to use the Rust-Oleum Painters Touch Ultra Cover Flat Gray Primer for the cockpit and front canopy frame. I’m going to use the Krylon Color Max Primer Gray Primer for the off color panel details around the fuselage. And I’m going to use the Rust-Oleum Gloss Protective Enamel Black for the Faux Koolshade panels.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/97b94546-f877-4991-a03d-c347f043c7a7-jpeg.1703167/]

I have a quick update to share.

I’m at the point where I need to suspend the models while I work on them, so I started fabricating the display bases. Using my tablet saw, I ripped some sheets of MDF board to size.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2320-jpeg.1970056/]

I used super glue to laminate the MDF sheets together. I prefer super glue to wood glue in this application because I have had MDF warp when using wood glue on prior projects. I made the bases thick and heavy so I don’t have to worry about the models toppling over, especially the Interceptor with the wings that extend and cantilever beyond the center line of the fuselage.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3454-jpeg.1970065/]

I’m painting the display bases with Rust-Oleum Oil Based Protective Enamel Gloss Black Paint. Rust-Oleum Oil Paint is excellent stuff, it sticks to everything including glass, plastic, metal and concrete, and when it dries it can be sanded and polished to a mirror finish. However, one of the downsides is it takes a few weeks to dry before it gets to the point where you can polish it.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3531-jpeg.1970066/]

Another downside to using Rust-Oleum Oil Paint is it has a strong odor, so I prefer to do the painting outside. I find it helpful and efficient to gang a few different projects together and do them all at the same time like an assembly line. I’m also painting a display base for a Studio Scale AT-ST model and two other models that are in the production pipeline.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3529-jpeg.1970072/]

I have a quick tip to share-

The cost of paint brushes, rollers and sponge applicators can be high when I’m working on a project like this. To keeps costs down and preserve my painting tools between coats of paint I store them inside the paint can submerged in the paint. I have stored my painting tools using this method for years on end in some cases, as long as I’m using high quality painting tools I’ve never had an issue with them deteriorating. I also save a ton of time and effort not having to clean them, and time is money.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3523-jpeg.1970079/]

Yesterday I was able to apply 5 coats of paint on the tops and bottoms and 10 coats on the sides. The sides always need extra paint coverage because it’s the sawn edge which is very porous.

Now it’s time for a little sanding.

This is not a finish sand, it’s to knock down any high points and see if there are any areas that need special attention. In the below photo there are some bubbles, runs and the seam where the sheets of MDF were laminated together.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3540-jpeg.1970317/]

Unfortunately I can’t use an electric sander for this because it moves too quickly and it will melt the paint, so I do it by hand with course 60 grit sandpaper. In the below photo the seam is almost gone, and the rest will fill in with a subsequent coat of paint.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3548-jpeg.1970318/]

I usually don't treat the face of MDF before painting, but for raw cut edges I always apply water-thin superglue to seal it. It really soaks in, so it generally takes at least 2 passes. It's best to do it with adequate ventilation too - as the glue rapidly cures and "cooks off" the fumes are nose-burning and eye-watering. 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]
 Let it sit for at least an hour to harden up and then sand it down smooth with a sanding block - works like a charm.
