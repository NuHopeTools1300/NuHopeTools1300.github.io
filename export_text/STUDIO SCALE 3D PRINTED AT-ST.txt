I did a little work on the AT-ST today. The legs on the model kit are not posable, and I decided to change the leg position to make the model a little taller. The legs on the left is how the kit is designed to fit together, and on the right how I want them to look.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2039-jpeg.1823555/]

I used a hobby saw to remove a portion of the “male” joint so the leg can rotate into the position I want.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2041-jpeg.1823556/]

I used a piece of scrap paper to make a template of the joint with the new angle.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2043-jpeg.1823557/]

Then I used the template to transfer the cut angle to the other part.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2045-jpeg.1823558/]

Here are the legs in their new position, and they still fit together with a snug friction fit despite the material I cut off.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2047-jpeg.1823559/]

Now that I have changed the angle of the knee joint, I also had to change the angle of the hip joint. To accomplish this I removed the “male” joint and filled in the “female” joint with Bondo.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2049-jpeg.1823564/]

I let the Bondo cure for a few days, then I installed a metal pin where the “male” joint was, and drilled a hole to accept the pin where the “female” joint was. Now the hip joint can rotate to any angle I want.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2080-jpeg.1823565/]

More to come soon. Thank you for looking.

Oops, I accidentally broke one of the parts.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2093-jpeg.1833138/]

I repaired the broken part with baking soda reinforced super glue. I also used baking soda and super glue to build up the thickness of the unbroken part so that both parts match.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2094-jpeg.1833141/]

While the baking soda and super glue were still wet, I shaped the parts with sanding sticks.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2095-jpeg.1833142/]

I let the baking soda and super glue cure for a few days then I gave the rebuilt areas a coat or Mr Hobby 1500 to even out the surface.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2096-jpeg.1833149/]

I primed the repaired parts with Rust-Oleum Painter’s Touch Ultra Cover Flat Gray Primer spray paint, which I decanted from the spray can and shot through my airbrush. I used a ratio 50% paint to 50% Testors Enamel Thinner at 60psi.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2158-jpeg.1833150/]

Here is the AT-ST test fitted together with the taller leg position. At this point nothing is glued and the model is held together with a friction fit or double sided tape. Now that the model is taller, its top heavy and it has to be supported by the aluminum rod.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2253-jpeg.1833151/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2272-jpeg.1833153/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2259-jpeg.1833154/]

The designer of the model kit designed the kit with a round hole on the bottom of the drive engine to accept a support rod. This is a very convenient feature and two thumbs up to the model kit designer. However, if you choose not to support the model in this way, there is a part that comes with the kit to cover up the hole.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_2368-jpeg.1833155/]

Hello Joberg; Thank you for the question.

I use hot water to shape the styrene rod. Specifically, I used a medium size bowl filled with tap water and I heated the bowl of water in the microwave on the high setting for 5-8 minutes. Then I dipped the styrene rod in the hot water to make the plastic pliable.

SAFETY DISCLAIMER- If you choose to use this method use extreme caution because there is a phenomenon called “superheating,” where water heated in a microwave exceeds the boiling point but doesn’t boil and the water can be explosive if it’s disturbed right after heating. That said, the water is so hot it makes the styrene plastic very soft and pliable very quickly.

I have a quick update to share-

I’m at the point where I need to work on the model while it’s assembled, so I started fabricating the display base. Using my table saw, I ripped some sheets of MDF board to size. Then I used super glue to laminate the MDF sheets together. I prefer super glue to wood glue in this application because I have had MDF warp when using wood glue on prior projects. I positioned the AT-ST on the display base where it appeared to be “aesthetically centered,” then I traced where the feet and a hole for the support post needs to be drilled.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3392-jpeg.1970080/]

I’m painting the display base with Rust-Oleum Oil Based Protective Enamel Gloss Black Paint. Rust-Oleum Oil Paint is excellent stuff, it sticks to everything including glass, plastic, metal and concrete, and can be sanded and polished to a mirror finish. However, one of the downsides is it takes a few weeks to dry to get to the point where you can polish it.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3531-jpeg.1970086/]

Another downside to using Rust-Oleum Oil Paint is it has a strong odor, so I prefer to do the painting outside. I find it helpful and efficient to gang a few different projects together and do them all at the same time like an assembly line. I’m also painting the display bases for a Studio Scale Tie Fighter and Tie Interceptor and two other models that are in the production pipeline.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3529-jpeg.1970091/]

Prior to starting the painting process, I drilled a small pilot hole where the support post will go. After the final coat of paint has been applied I’ll drill the hole to the correct diameter and depth on my drill press.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3534-jpeg.1970092/]

I have a quick tip to share-

The cost of paint brushes, rollers and sponge applicators can be high when I’m working on a project like this. To keep costs down and preserve my painting tools between coats of paint I store them inside the paint can submerged in the paint. I have stored my painting tools using this method for years on end in some cases, as long as I’m using high quality tools I’ve never had an issue with them deteriorating. I also save a ton of time and effort not having to clean them, and time is money.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_3523-jpeg.1970094/]

Yesterday I was able to apply 5 coats of paint to the tops and bottoms and 10 coats to the sides. The sides always need extra paint coverage because it’s the sawn edge which is very porous.
