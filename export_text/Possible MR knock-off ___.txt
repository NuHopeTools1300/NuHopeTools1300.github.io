Comon man, you know that's oversimplifying things.  The whole hobby is an ethical grey area for the most part.  If you love a movie, and want a reporoduction of  a ship or a prop from that movie that has never been offered, and yuo go through the time and effort to build a model, or a prop yourself most of us would agree that is perfectly fine, but technically, it's someone else's idea, their intellectual property that you have re-created.  So even scratch builds could be offensive to someone.

The problem is when financial gain becomes a factor.  A lot of things slide because their are such a small niche that there isn't enough of a market for them to justify the trouble of selling the rights and developing a product, and marketting, and packaging and retailing and etc., etc., etc.  

Then when you get licensing agreements for specific items involved you have to step back a bit, because at that point someone, who owns the rights to the property has decided that they deserve to reap the benefits of their creation, or the creation to which they own the rights. Generally if you want to stay on the right side of things, you'd stay away from selling, for profit, reproductions of licensed works, you sure as hell would want to stay away from directly reproducing a product developed and marketted by an officially licensed company.

That doesn't mean everyone has common sense.  

However, is a guy making a reproductions of a cult movie weapon that has never been reproduced and selling copies to fans who like himself love the flick and want to have a reproduction of the movie prop, for a meager profit a criminal?  I'd say no, but if he makes enough money whoever owns that IP will decide that yes, indeed he is.

Well the whole idea of kit cars it to lower the price, reverse engineering the whole car would be cost prohibitive...  But...

Engine and drive lines are usually replaced with easier to source more conventional, reliable and easy to work on systems...  Chevy engines are probably the most popular they are usually coupled to Porsche transaxles for use in rear or mid engine cars with rear wheel drive...  Corvette, Mustang and even Camaro front suspensions are common now as the Fieros are now entering antique automobile classification...

But yes for some vintage cars like say the Shelby Cobra, there are people out there making bootleg 427 blocks, hell even Shelby himself is producing an aluminum 427 knock off engine...

For emblems, badges, headlights and glass they are usually sourced as replacement parts for the real car, but again there are some companies out there that have started to produce their own knock-off versions, just like there are knock off fenders made in Japan for just about every car...

Back on the subject of Ferrari there were a ton of replica 308s made back when the Fiero was out, the Fiero was a poor man's Ferrari and slapping a replica 308 body on it was nearly painless...  Upgrade to a Cadillac Northstar V8 and modify the suspension in the Fiero and it was compariable to the Ferrari in most performance aspects...  They were even stretching the Fiero frames and making Lambos back then, they still are but tube frames are more common...

Like all re-casting topics, this one has degenerated from "Should re-casting be OK?" to "This guy (fill in the blank) is a creep".

My take on re-casting items made by major makers is: These items were, in most cases, relatively expensive. Many people spent money they couldn't really afford to obtain them. They proudly display the items as part of their collection, knowing they have purchased real, authentic, licensed items. Then re-casters start flooding the market with knock-offs. Without question the original buyers items have been de-valued. Now, these items are not meant to be investments, per se; but they are nonetheless. The knock-offs have rendered the originals hundreds of dollars less in value. Now, it can be argued that an original, because of its originality, is worth more than a re-cast, but the market says "no". The average collector will fill a hole in their collection with a knock-off in a heartbeat. The budget buyer will jump over all the expensive, licensed items to get an example of a particular item, without a qualm. They might even get a spurious Certificate of Authenticity to go with it. That de-values all the real ones even more!  To me that is one of the real tragedies of re-casting.

Update: I think I may have posted this reply in the wrong topic:cry But my opinion still stands!
