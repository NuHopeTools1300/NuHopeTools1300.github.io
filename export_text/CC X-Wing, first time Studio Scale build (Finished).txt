Re: CC X-Wing, first time Studio Scale build!!!!

Guy your nose cone is always this way with most of these kits, they all require a little fettling here and there.

Best way for the nose to fit well, is once you have a good alligned join on your hull halves and the nose is "almost" there, youll normally have a gap at the curvature area?

What i do is paste a "sheet" of epoxy putty there, then push on the nose cone and glue.

From there, wet sculpt the putty into the contours, pretty much so all you have is a smooth line of putty, that will require the least amount ( i really do mean tiny) of sanding. This area can be a pig if you have gobs of filler in there, to sculpt it in, use a paint brush and apply water to the putty, gently smoothing it out!

The other area that can test, is the buttplate, you may need to force the hull halves (wing block apertures) apart, to do this warm the hull so the resin becomes plyable, from there, attach the plate to the top hull.

The lower hull, i force out with a bolt with two plates at either end, you can turn the bolt and line up the hull with the lower of the buttplate, and glue!

Thats the simple way, lol you may need the same approach in putty and maybe a re scribe once all sanded, but itll get there!

Hope that helps, and of course, this board is full of guys that know tons, so just ask away!

Re: CC X-Wing, first time Studio Scale build!!!!

Regarding pastels, you don't really need to coat if BEFORE you apply, but you should clear coat it after to seal the powder in.  

In terms of applications, there are many ways to do it.  Personally, I grind the powder off the chalk stick with sand paper, then I use a fairly short-bristled brush and just rub the pastels in to the paint with the brush.  

You need to be careful though because the amount of powder that is carried on the brush and hence transfered to the model is a bit challenging to control - sometimes you'll roll the brush in a LOT of powder and barely any transfers; sometimes you think you have barely any on the brush and it goes on WAY too dark and heavy.  Go with a light touch!

You can also smudge it on with your fingers, or with a damp cloth.

Another thought regarding pastels is that - even though it's not paint - it can be very difficult to remove, especially if its well rubbed into the paint.  Again, go light!  You can always build it up more as you go.  And practice on another throw away model if you can.

If I may offer one recommendation, or bit of feedback,  on your model -- everything is looking great so far, and I know you'll be doing more, but the yellow and the green are coming across as very rich in color.  You might experiment with rubbing these sections down with ultra fine sand paper (1200 grit or higher), or using a very light colored pastel over the secions.  I've used baby powder for white to lighten areas; works moderatley well, though not a silver bullet.

Re: CC X-Wing, first time Studio Scale build!!!!

Thanks Lee...

Guy, I forgot to comment on the panel lines.  

In my experience, the challenge with panel lines is not so much HOW you darken them, but how dark you make them.  Too light and it's looses it's effect.  Too dark and it looks funny.  

Again, experiment if you can.  Either on a scrap model, or on the X itself, but with a water-based paint that can easily be removed if too dark.  And I think it's plainly obvvious, but pastels of course are not the way to go.  You need a liquid medium to flow down the lines (I'm sure you know this though).  

I actually tried using a pencil, because I liked the "dark gray, not quite black" tone, but it's not really a great technique because it really highlights the surfact texture of the paint, even in those tight grooves, and looks "pebbly".

One more comment on applying pastels.  I mentioned no special treatment before applying them.  That is granted that you have a flat coat on the model.  If you have gloss paints (base coats or otherwise) the pastel powder will have less to adhere to, as the painted surface will have less "teeth".

Easy Answer:  Take photos.  Things ALWAYS look different in pictures.  You will always be able to see things that for some mysterious reason you don't notice in person.  Even my own children - I have picture of the two of them sitting together, and in studying the photo, I see that I've never really noticed how much they really do look like brother and sister.  Colors will strike you differently, weathering details will appear too light or too dark, all sorts of things pop out.

Difficult Answer:  In my untrained opinion, the mark of a true artist is his eye.  Or in other words, how the individual artist views and interprets a subject, and his own personal views and opinions of what is tastefull, good, or correct.  As an example, an artist's technical abilities, or even his tangible artistic skill are not going to make much difference if the artist fails to realize that X-Wing stripes are red and Viper stripes are orange.  So not only do you need the skill and technique to paint and weather well, you need the subjective eye to know what is needed, what you want, what is good, and what is right...  Make sense?

Re: CC X-Wing, first time Studio Scale build!!!!

No problem - my pleasure!  And just to clarify (if even necessary) my stripe color example was just the first thing that popped into mind as an example.  Don't know what color you are using for the red, but it looks SPOT on!

In the United States, we have Testors Clear Flat, and Testor's Dull Coat.  They're essentially identical; just a clear, flat spray paint.  It has adhered to anything I've sprayed it on, and goes on super thin.  It's never attacked anything I've sprayed it on.  Not sure if you can get it over there.

Caustic thinners may attack it if heavily applied though, or paints with strong solvents (Floquil is one :unsure).  So with washes of oil-based enamels (where I presume you're using thinner as your wash medium), go slow and easy, and don't keep hitting the same area over and over again, (or you'll turn your base coat into a muddy mess).
