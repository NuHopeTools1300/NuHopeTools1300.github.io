I used 4 colors of primer.

1. Gray

2. Red Oxide

3. Black

4..... and white as the color coat. That's right! Primers. The white can be sanded, buffed, etc. It takes pastels well too. Those yellow panels on Red Five and Red Two that I did? Pastels. Nothing more. The reds were either thinned craft paint shot thru my Iwata, or Barn Red spray paint. Then all of this and the weathering effects were sealed with clear fixative or Krylon Matt clear. Depending on the spray concentration, the sheen would vary from dead flat to an almost metallic semigloss. Some of the panels had Future floor acrylic brush painted on for random sheens and reflectivity.

This really isn't hard. You just have to be willing to think unconventionally and use what works for you. I also made washes that were applied with a brush or dappled on with a sea sponge.

I'm old school. I don't really give a **** what I use, as long as the desired result is achieved and the results are durable.

Don't let anything stop you. Build. Paint. Enjoy.

^ Yeah Rob... those are tough to get right. When I made my templates, I had to use 'landmarks' on the wings like panel lines and such to plot how wide the stripes would be and so forth.

I cut this template out of printer paper, then I laid that on the wings, then lightly marked where the masking tape would go with a mechanical pencil. For opposite sides, I merely flipped the template over. Masking these is a little tedious but it'll work out ok. 

I used the same template for Red Two, but obviously didn't use all five stripes. On the Red Two filming model, the stripes vary in width and length, so I masked those by eye once I had the main mark established.

Someone asked me once for measurements on those markings, but was probably appalled when I said I 'eyeballed' it. But that's the truth! Sometimes you just have to put the calipers aside and just paint. Otherwise nothing gets done. And if it turns out wrong, you have to ask yourself how much error you want to live with and if it's worth it to correct if the error is slight and only you will know about it.

I think that Farm Implement Red was an Ernst brand...LOL!!!

I figured with the amount of damage I was going to do to the markings, it didn't matter so much if it was Floquil or Tamiya or Wally-World Apple Barrel as long as the overall shade of the paint was close to correct.

I don't get hung up on trying to do exact color matches because of several factors:

1. Weathering

2. Differences in film stock

3. Different lighting techniques in photography and filming

4. Different perceptions of color unique to each one of us as individuals

5. Different monitors being adjusted differently

6. Aging of the models being replicated and subsequent aging of the weathering effects.

7. Variations in paint color not only from lot to lot from the SAME manufacturer, but also, as you mentioned, changes in formulations over the decades from that manufacturer. ( Product features and specifications may be subject to change without notice at any time. )

My main criteria are:

1. Does it look right to me or to my patron ?

2. Is it durable enough to stand up to years of being handled/displayed ?

If so, then I've done my job without giving myself unneeded headaches due to trivialities. Now... lest I be misunderstood, if a person wants to do EVERYTHING exactly as it was done in the old days... meaning materials and methods, the by god... do it and have fun! I think that's great.

I'm working my way through the weathering on my FX conversion.  I started with a white base to sort of test this approach.

It's funny how ambient lighting effects this model.  In my shop, which is nearly all white flourescent light, the model registers almost too white, even in areas of heavy weathering.

But under more yellow, incandescent lighting, it looks more correct.

Even Kuhn Global's Red 5 looks different under the different ambient conditions.  With his blues screen shots, its more white. and with the green screeen shots it looks more beige.
