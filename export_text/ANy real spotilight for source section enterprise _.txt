the only way to get a true spot from the bridge is to raise the bridge high enough to get the light over the B/C deck section. Lighting from the inside is called the "raytheon" method. It does work, but.. it's not very easy to get the proper look(light edges falling off properly, shadows from the phasers, light showing through the decals, etc). On my build I'm using 100ma SMD leds. I still have to raise the bridge a little, but not as much as 3mm or 5mm leds would require. I'll be posting pics of what I'm doing on my build soon.

On the motion pictures some of the floods were secondary above light sources, not actually in the model.

I went looking through a couple of notebooks and technical manuals on the original miniatures to see if there were any clues.

As has been mentioned before, this model was nearly completed by the time RA&A left the film.  When Doug Trumbull came in, he made some revisions including the whole "self lit" concept, which necessitated a revised bridge and a revised planetary dome (on the underside of the saucer).

This memo shows that as of January 1979, they were just getting started on those changes - 

[IMAGE: attachment.php]

The wiring diagrams don't reveal anything about the spotlights (location, voltage, etc) leading me to think this diagram was drawn prior to the addition of the spotlights.

But this page offered up a new mystery (check out the highlighted text) - 

[IMAGE: attachment.php]

"Rear" Photon torpedo?  Huh?  Didn't know the refit Enterprise had a rear photon torpedo tube.

And it's not a typo. On the next page, they list the forward facing photon torpedo tubes - 

[IMAGE: attachment.php]

So, where the hell is the rear facing photon torpedo tube?  I mean, it exists - it has a light - but where is it?

All of these lights were pretty low voltage, save for the neon.  And it should be obvious from the time period that LED's were not used.  They could have put little glass lenses over the lights and buried the bulbs in lengths of tubing to get them to focus.   and there are some incandescent bulbs that have little glass lenses at the tip.  But remember that their appearance on film is due to both the brightness of the bulb and the exposure time of the film.  So, trying to build something that looks right to your eye using what you are used to seeing on film is going to be tricky.......
