Tamiya - 1/35 Chieftain Mk.5

Steve,

These are the kits that have the donor parts to make a new butt.

Tamiya - 1/35 German 4-wheeled Armored Car Sd.Kfz.222 (TAM35051)

[IMAGE: tam35051box.gif]

You will need the body to make the tub that you see above. You will have to modify it like ILM did to round off the bottom.

Tamiya - 1/35 Soviet T-34/76 1942 Tank (TAM35049)

[IMAGE: tam35049box.gif]

You will need the upper section of the turret including the commanders hatch. You will also need one of the rear engine grills (the kit brings 2) and you will also need the inside part that attaches to the drivers hatch.

Tamiya - 1/35 British Chieftain Mk.V Tank (TAM3506

[IMAGE: icon_cool.gif]

[IMAGE: tam35068box.gif]

You will need a suspension section off this kit which is the "T" shaped part with the springs behind it that you see in the pic above.

There's a small grilled part I'm still looking for that is also added in th butt tub.

Hope this helps out.

Rolando

The undercarriage of the real walkers are very different from the kit. The real ones had a inner structure built inside which held the hip cylinders and the drive trains. The plastic hull (which was hollow) was then just placed over the inner "skeleton". I'm going to try and replicate all this on my build up but in order to do that most of the kit hull has to be rebuilt. For a start you have to cut away the entire bottom of the hull and then custom make the entire inner structure from scratch.

In any case, you don't need to ID these greeblies to build up the structures of your AT-AT hull, head, and legs (it is, of course, helpful to know the size of a few key kit parts for proper scaling, but there's enough info in this thread to obtain some of the major ones). It sounds like your plans are fairly mature. Go ahead and polish them up, get the vintage kits you know you'll need, and start building! 

Post plenty of in-progress photos as you go (we all love seeing those), and then, when you hit a snag, I bet you'll find plenty of fellow SS modelers ready to lend a hand. It's just a matter of plunging in and showing you're serious about your project.

The legs of Walker as designed can't collapse fully beneath it - the cutaway leg's enabled them to cheat the crashed pose.

The scale question is a difficult one to answer because the actual size is under some debate. I think that most numbers put it at around 74 feet tall...

http://www.theforce.net/swtc/walkers.html#atat

If you assume that number is correct, I guess you can just take the measurements of the studio scale props and do the math.

A lot of Star Wars craft are hard to nail down for an exact scale measurement. For the purposes of composition they change size in comparison to other objects from shot to shot in the films. Even the craft built as full size set pieces can be inconsistent as the the exterior sets are often too small to accommodate the interior sets of the same vehicles.
