I'm building the tenth and final pyro casting made by the late Dave Jones (as offered by BrundelFly). It's one of those "ILMer who took blown up parts home" deals.

I'm pretty certain he recreated many of the smaller pieces in later years so he could finish out the model. Thus I'm not worrying too much about the blasphemy of replacing parts as needed, as they might not be legacy anyway.

So I started small, using real Hanomag parts for the rear wing recess, and replaced a damaged C-channel with a real piece of Plastruct.

moffeaton posted on fb that all four Saturn stage halves (part 43) were the same on most pyros, omitting the one with the long rectangular ridge (part 44), so I took a fresh look at what I had. My kit came with a single part 44 can.

Well, I didn't buy two vintage Saturn V's for nuthin! And as long as I was scratching one, I might as well do two so they match. One of my castings has a bubble in an unfortunate spot anyway, as seen in the first pic (I wasn't looking forward to my hand-rescribing giving the patch away).

Doing a clean job of it is another matter. I'm impressed and also pissed off by moffeaton's pristine masters! Next is hitting it with primer to reveal all the sins. Naturally, I'm putting that off, as I'm not ready for the ugly truth quite yet.

Also did the flat inserts, cut styrene flat platforms, and added vintage Saturn rocket thingies.

The kit has cast posts on both the rear of the cans AND the engines/exhausts. One or the other had to go. I ended up removing them on both and going with my own attachment rods. Yes... GASP... they're wood. They were handles on disposable foam paint brushes. I save everything remotely hobby-applicable. I'm not lighting it, so there was no need to use hollow brass or whatever. What little will be exposed will be very hard to see so I'm not worried about detectable wood grain.

I have two Phantoms, the Panther, the Elefant, and the Whitley, but not the U-boat, so I am spared from the unnecessary work of reinventing the wheel by kitbashing two of the engines/exhausts (like I inflicted upon myself with the Saturn halves).

The seams on the join of the exhaust halves are driving me crazy, but I'm trying to let it go. They are terrible in every reference photo I can find, even the heros.

Declaring my Saturn cans have reached a sufficiently presentable state.

The kit comes with resin heat sinks, but I have my own. They're supposed to have a ring around them, though, so I cut some. The ring should be thick enough to be flush with the ID of the turkey feathers, but unless I find another tube of the correct dims, I'm going with this.

I've gone back and forth on whether the sinks should be inset down within the exhaust cans (am I calling these parts the right thing?), or within the space of the feathers. I've finally decided the former makes them too deep. But I welcome opinions....on anything.

Wing attachment.

I want the wings removable for transport. And even if I was gluing them, I needed pegs.

I feel there isn't enough meat in the edge of the wing that mates with the fuselage. But the solid resin cast engine halves would make a good foundation. I have some steel rod stock lying around, that will be my pegs.

So I drilled! I made a styrene template so I could make sure the equivalent holes on the fuselage would match up. (in shadow kind of hanging off the edge in the first pic)

I can't believe it worked without some kind of disaster to correct (which is my usual M.O.). Solid as a rock.

On a custom model, I would have no problem with the pegs being visible. Functional detailing hiding in plain sight.

For the X-Wing, I will cover them with Sealab tanks. They belong on heros, not pyros, but..

Edit: I'm having to redo the holes on the fuselage after all LOL

Over 20 years ago, my intent was to make a hero X-Wing from scratch, so I started amassing kits and materials.

I never did get ALL the kits, but came close. But I ain't getting any younger, and have, with no regrets, abandoned the scratch hero plan. This pyro will be my "forever" X-Wing.

....but.... I have all those kits! So I'm using them as opportunity arises, even though the resin parts are perfectly fine.

Thus the time has come to turn down some Hummel rollers with my... um.. "lathe", which is to say, by chucking them in my power drill and spinning them against an emery board.

I also have pegged the cannon bases as a mechanical assist to the glue.

This cannon experienced unobserved slippage before the glue dried in two ways:

1. The heat sinks are not aligned so that the unique tines around the round bit line up with the as-yet unmounted brass rods. Not worrying about it.

2. The thickest brass tube isn't flush with the end of the next-smaller segment. But IT WILL BE OKAY, because:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screen-shot-2025-09-21-at-12-20-44-pm-png.1971985/]

it just so happens that Red 12 has painted stripes along that portion of its cannons, and my mistake more or less aligns with the rear edge of that stripe. So the edge of the improperly placed tube will become the edge of the stripe, making the step down much less noticeable.

Moving on!

While I waited for my new red paint, I worked on the base.

In recent years I've mostly preferred minimalist and practical display bases: just a chunk of plywood painted black. I feel elaborate bases call attention to themselves and away from the model.

But back, once again, to 25 or so years ago when I took the first steps toward SW SS, the now-departed SgtFang offered recasts of ICONS bases derived from Death Star tiles. I grabbed three. So it's not like I'm not going to use them now.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260102_223722-jpg.2001745/]

They're wavy and wonky and full of bubble holes. Other than making sure it sits fairly level, I did nothing about any of that. I did, though, dig out dozens of boogers and wash it.

I cemented in a piece of the same aluminum tubing that I entombed inside the X-Wing, to receive the steel support rod I've prepared.

I know moffeaton formulated a great mixing recipe for DS tile matches, but as I've stated before, I'm going with rattle cans as much as I can--I'm not going to go all crazy airbrushing a base. I even considered just leaving it all gray, considering my worries about over-done bases.

I primered in black, then put on a dark gray (Rustoleum or something) that is also a primer. Too dark, so I put on yet another primer that's a little lighter. That ended up being close enough for me for the darker tiles.

I started to tape them off, and paint was coming right off. This resin! So I had to gate off the dark tiles with pieces of cardboard...

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260104_133619-jpg.2001746/]

...and spray the rest with Tamiya Surface Primer.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260104_134654-jpg.2001747/]

Then another round of gating off for the yellow/green bits, for which I used Tamiya TS-3 Dark Yellow.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260104_163816-jpg.2001748/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260104_164838-jpg.2001749/]

(these outdoor pics were taken on a dreary, overcast, post-rainstorm day)

In this case I don't care about a few spots of overspray or insufficient coverage.

As I was finishing up, the darker/duller red I ordered arrived. Oops, I neglected to make sure it was a rattle can!

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/20260104_171144-jpg.2001750/]

Well, I guess I'll be using the airbrush earlier than I expected. This color was selected not because I think it's a perfect ILM match, but because I think/hope that if misted sparingly, it will nudge my existing paint job toward that direction. To blend with the existing passes rather than cover them.

So I went ahead and painted the wing stripes, with the two shades of red I used before on the fuselage, just so the procedure is the same and they'll match. I will mist on the new red in the coming days. I think it's pretty close already, so it won't take much to satisfy my eye (no accuracy claims here).

MY BEEF WITH AIRBRUSHING: A Confessional

20+ years ago my experience with airbrushing was so frustrating I hadn't touched one again until today.

Back then, I used canned compressed air, which was expensive, then a little compressor, which was expensive, but my main complaint was cleaning the airbrush.

You see, it would get all gummed up and I'd have to take it completely apart and clean it with mineral spirits or something, and then reassemble all the little rods and valves and stuff in the innards. And it would clog again quickly.

Only today did I realize something: I hadn't been using thinner. I was spraying rattle can paint directly into the cup.

My attitude about airbrushes all these years have been based on my ignorant usage of them.

Since then, I have absorbed via osmosis the right way to do it, from all the model building videos I watch.

So today, I did it right, with a cheap but capable unit with its own rechargeable compressor incorporated as a handle, and cleanup was a breeze.

Airbrushes, I have let go of my hate, and I hope you forgive me.

Current state of my stripes, which started as passes of Tamiya PS-2 and PS-34 rattle cans, and now misted over with LP-18 (from a bottle!). Wow, when they labeled it as matte they weren't kidding! It's okay, there will be at least one complete clear coat of the model before the end.

Is it accurate, probably not, but I've gotten rid of the candy apple look, which was the goal.

Project summary:

Dave Jones resin pyro kit

Jason's painting masks and decals and cannon brass

base: recast of old ICONS base

real or styrene parts used in place of resin ones (which were fine, but hey):

  - Phantom turkey feathers

  - M8 Carrier wheels

  - Hummel rollers (turned down for cannons)

  - two of the Saturn V cans (70s tooling)

  - 6 of the 8 engine can intake Saturn greeblies  (70s tooling)

  - Hanomag parts for wing trailing trenches

  - styrene rod stock for those trenches (Dave actually did cast up resin ones, bless 'im. I would've used brass but had Plastruct stock on hand and it worked fine)

  - most of the butt plate parts (sans Hummel)

  - real heat sinks

Hours spent: No idea, but enough that ILM would've fired me

Rear engines: 3
