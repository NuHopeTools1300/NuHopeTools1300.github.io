IMO this thread needs to be bumped as I see more and more questions on the forum about what an accurate 32" studio scale Millenium Falcon replica would even look like. 

Well, here you go folks! Want to know what that miniature really looked like under all of that paint? Julien's build is just one excellent example that has been posted to this forum over the years. His is also most certainly one of the best, again, IMO.

And if you want your cockpit to look like the actual filming model, rather than the one you got along with your kit, then you might want to consider politely asking Julien what donor kits he used for his, because his cockpit has actually been thoroughly researched, carefully and lovingly recreated and is exceptionally accurate!

Don't really care how accurate your model is or what anyone here thinks about it? Prefer the look of the full-size cockpit set to the miniature version? Well that's all fine too, obviously. It's your model, so build it however you want. This is a hobby, and it's supposed to be fun, right?

Just be skeptical, though, when anyone tells you that his model or any other model is "99-percent accurate". Because unless he's kicking it like Julien and some of the other experienced researchers, scratchbuilders, and super-talented builders here, who have been building replicas of these models the same way the originals were built, there's simply no way his model is going to be anything close to 99-percent accurate.

Again, all IMO.

I wish I had been a member here back then because the fans know a lot more about these models than most of the employees do anymore. 

Admittedly they were built "a long time ago" and we seldom paid attention to what kit the parts come out of. If it looked cool - it got glued on!

There were some missing parts on the Falcon that we didn't replace, because we didn't have the time or budget to track them down. If I'd known all of you back then, it would have been easy!

The little stuff is just for visual break up. Movie models work on the concept of implied detail. By putting small bits here and there, the viewer's eye picks up on it and their brain fills it in, making sense out of what they perceive is there. The viewer's mind will even create detail that isn't even there. The details make it look like there is more going on than there really is.

We try to apply details to models so that they seem to make visual sense, but we leave it to the fans to decide what they all are. I love seeing visual dictionary names for all the bits and bobs that are on these models.

Reactions : Like — MonsieurTox, -... . .- --..
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

That's right. Most filming miniatures look great on screen but horrible in person. 

Because of budgets and time constraints, we don't get to indulge in making the models as clean as we'd like to. The model gets done well enough to look good while zipping across the screen for 2 seconds. The glue globs hide amongst the rest of the details, and the high contrast weathering has to be overdone so that it reads on film for that brief shot and doesn't get blown out by the bright studio lights. 

Not to mention that the model gets touched up all through the shooting process. By the time the shooting schedule is over, the "original" paint job has been touched up, painted over and had details added and subtracted. Movies don't get shot in order either, so that's why details can appear, disappear and then reappear throughout a movie.

That's Movie Magic !!!

Foobos, the basic issue is this:  limited (or non-existant) supply of the donor kits has fostered a reticence to share part identifications within the studio scale (SS) fraternity for fear of worsening the availability of said donor kits.  This is not an intuitive paradigm but one that I also had to become educated of when I first joined the forum.  Because of the frequency of such requests as yours, there is a certain level of annoyance for the long time SS practitioners.  Also, as donor parts identification & research usually involves a ridiculous amount of effort, the perception of someone asking for a 'freebie shortcut' without any demonstrated commitment is offensive or at the least, seen as disrespectful.

I relay this in hope that it helps resolve any misunderstanding but my experience is my own and I obviously cannot speak for any other members.

Hello all

Was at the Munich show yesterday...and of course saw the "holy grail" there as well! What first struck me was the sharpness of the surface details, of course created by using seperate parts rather then a one piece mold as most models have it..even Bandai. Lighting in the room was crap...very dark but then of course needed like that to protect the artefects. I did spot some recent damage to the small Falcon...the left side Sherman wheel on the engine deck is gone leaving a blank spot now. Also the plating around the engine seems to start lifting off a bit. Glue marks around the top gun mount were also visible, not sure if they are recent or from way back when? All in all the show was good even though I think they should now overhaul the concept to integrate the TFA characters into the game part. In Munich they have only three TFA exibits, a FO storm trooper, BB8 and a rebel flight helmet. My personal highlights were...the Falcon of course and then Slave1 which looked magnificent!

cheers

Uwe

PS: Yes I spotted the pin up but it's very hard to see with the naked eye...;-)
