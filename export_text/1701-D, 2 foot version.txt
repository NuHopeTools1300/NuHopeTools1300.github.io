They're done on a resin printer at i.materialise.  Finding a high-rez printer that can do large prints has opened up a lot of possibilities for me.  They advertise 50 micron resolution but do such a good job setting up that things I've gotten from them look as good as 16 micron prints from others.  Never had anything done on an FDM printer as I'm not sure the sesolution would be sufficient...too bad since I could buy a good quality FDM printer with what this will cost to have printed in resin.  This model will cost in the neighborhood of $4k to print...if I could find a printer for that price that would handle parts this big, work in the resolution I need, but not require months of fiddling and adjusting in order to produce large pieces of similar qulaity to what's shown in the above images, I'd seriously considering buying it.

As you can see in the pics, this part has no grain or layering worth speaking of.  There were a few glitches and some details were left out but problems were expected...i.materialise advertises minimum details of .5mm for this material, but I made this model with most fine details (window depth, phaser emitter depth, grid lines) at .2mm, assuming they allowed a margin of error that could be pushed.  For the most part, it worked out.

Regarding modeling: yeah, I'll stick with what I know, but sometimes LW brings out my temper...while finishing up the saucer bottom this morning, cleaning up the last few mesh errors seemed to be creating new errors for me to fix.  At one point I was literally shouting at the top of my lungs and smashing my mouse on the desktop...must've drove my blood pressure sky high as I was seeing bright flare spots in my field of vision.  Thank God I use a near-indestructible IBM model M keyboard as that has been mercilessly pounded at times.  Perhaps true CAD software would work better for this application but such hysterical-fit-causing incidents are rare.

For me, it's a question of time vs. money...when I had the pieces printed on my Reliant project, several months were spent cleaning them up to make them suitable as masters.
