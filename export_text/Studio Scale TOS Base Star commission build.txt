This is the commission build for the client.  I’m sorry it’s taken so long to get this thread started.  Why not get it started on a day off!  Thanks to everyone for their questions.  I hope this answers your questions and guides those who have purchased kits already.

  The “inner pie” was the most challenging of the masters.  It started with 2 pics form the Prop Store auction a few years ago.  I’m sure many of you have them.  After IDing everything, I scanned the greeblies and started a 2D render in Rhino.  See below.  I used the 2D outlines to keep the file size down.

  Once the 2D map was done, I rendered it in 3D thanks to a series of measurements taken from the real part.  Most of what I did was really close, but this confirmed the measurements and made it perfect.  I added a tell into the structure, which is nearly impossible to tell and certainly impossible to fix.  

  I’m making it really easy to compare with the pics.

  I’ve taken pics of most of the masters.  There may seem like missing greeblies, but I kept them off, to be placed as add-ons to keep the rubber use down or if they looked like bubble-trappers (even when pressure cast).

  The inner pie ended up being constructed from 6 pieces.  This allowed uniform thickness and minimized the amount of resin and therefore the weight.  Doing it this way prevented having to do an open faced pour so some areas didn’t have to be really thick for others to have a minimum thickness.  It also allowed bubbles to be knocked out of any areas predisposed to trap them.  

  The 6 pieces for the inner pie fit together perfectly.  Minimal finishing of the seams were required.

  Whatever could be pressure cast was cast that way.  I’m really happy that the only bubbles were on parts kept for the prototype; there were only 3 bubbles on that.

  More to come.  Thanks for looking.

Thanks for the kind comments, everybody.

I’m going to illustrate this build as if it were a kit build.  Those of you who have purchased kits can follow along here, but it’s pretty much the same as shown on the PDF Instruction file you get with the kit.  I don't want it to sound like it's instructions for idiots.  If you’re building  already, please chime in!

I’m going to start with the support post.  If you start here, there will be less swearing and you’ll be happier later on.  I’ll go pic by pic.

First, drill 3 evenly spaced holes 20mm below the top rim.  These will be for set screws – I used 10-24 x 3/8, with a 11/64 pilot.

There are male (pic 2, on the R) and female (on the L) speaker jack assemblies included with the kit.  The metal part will be secured inside the central metal tube of the armature.

Feed the wire through the ABS cuff and place that in the top of the post.  Solder the leads to the female ¼” speaker jack assembly.  If you’re using Al/Cu wire, the Cu goes on the positive.

Take the positive jack, which is embedded in resin, insert it in the female jack and jam the whole thing so that the metal is flush.

Finish the pilot hole, tap it and place the set screws.

Bond the acrylic disc to the ABS cuff and that’s it.  I used thin weld-on.  Your model will be able to rotate freely without tangling the wire!

A little more pre-assembly.  This will make life easier later on.

  I masked and painted the “open” areas on each of the upper and lower cores.  There are acrylic alignment rings that come with these sections – adding little nubbies when its inverted ensures proper placement and won't sink in to far when you’re sealing everything later on.

The main discs are held in place on the central metal/ABS core with ABS cuffs.  Proper placement of these things is critical.

I placed the core on a scrap piece of wood with a 2” hole drilled through it.  The central core, an alignment ring then the lower core (with the 5 sprayed areas) is placed, then a main disc is put on top of that, with another alignment ring.

A cuff is forced down, then can be secured with thin weld-on.  This is an “inner cuff.”  ABS cement can also be used for this cuff, but placement can be really messy.

Repeat with the middle core, but you have to force it down with an alignment ring and main disc.  Secure it with weld-on after you’ve removed it.  It’s kinda mundane, but ends up being exact.

Thanks for looking.

Here, I lightly sanded the surface of the middle core, then traced out the areas covered by other parts, using the template (included with the kit).  It has the location for the FO trunks.  The outline represents the limit of greeblie placement.

  So I added the greeblies, masked and painted, as well as sprayed the insides black, to prevent any light leaks (leaving a clean 3mm rim on the inside, for fusing of the alignment rings later on).  It's pretty basic stuff so far.
