May 1975 to August 1975

Foundation

The Van Nuys site goes from idea to working effects base.

May 28, 1975

ILM is formally created

George Lucas signs `Industrial Light & Magic` into existence, establishing the frame for the entire ANH miniature program.

Site Exact
May to June 1975

The Van Nuys warehouse is found and leased

John Dykstra secures the future model-shop and shooting-stage site on `Valjean Avenue`.

Site Range
June to July 1975

The empty shell becomes a functioning shop

Model, machine, optical, office, and stage spaces are built out in a compressed burst of physical setup.

Site Range
July 1975

Dykstraflex design is completed

The motion-control system reaches a usable design state, which matters for almost every miniature that follows.

July to August 1975

Key departments and people come online

Art, optical, and animation start to take shape, with Johnston, Blalack, Beckett, and others locking the internal structure in place.

First Builds

August 1975

Paul Huston joins during the early scramble

He starts on boards with Joe Johnston before becoming one of the most useful eyewitnesses for the model-shop phase.

Site Exact-ish
September 25, 1975

Escape Pod blueprint is dated

This is one of the earliest precise dates in the entire ANH miniature corpus and anchors Joe Johnston directly to a design artifact.

Ships Exact
September to December 1975

Buildable X-wings are engineered in Van Nuys

The shop moves from concept drawings toward shootable miniature logic: structure, armature, geometry, and wing mechanics.

Fighters Range
December 1975

The first ILM Y-wing exists as England reference

The early ship later called `Red Jammer` is already real and travels incomplete to support the full-size build pipeline.

Fighters Range
December 26, 1975

Blue 1 is completed and shipped to John Barry

The first finished ILM X-wing becomes a bridge object between Van Nuys miniature work and Elstree cockpit/set reference.

January 1976 to June 1976

Reference to Stage

The reference objects come home, tests happen, and the real filming-era miniature program ignites.

January to February 1976

Blue 1 returns and becomes Red 2

The X-wing is completed with cockpit, pilot, and electrical finishing and is redressed into a proper ANH hero state.

Fighters Range
March 6, 1976

Maroon TIE test is photographed

A dated comp-test wedge proves active TIE stage work and reminds us the final grey look was not the only look in play.

Fighters Exact
March 22, 1976

Principal photography begins

By the time live-action shooting starts in Tunisia, Van Nuys is already active on miniature and motion-control work.

Spring 1976

Y-wing Number One master pattern is built

Paul Huston is tied to the master pattern or hero buck from which the ANH Y-wing family is cast.

Fighters Range

Death Star cannon anchors the first completed ILM shot

Huston and Stuart Ziff tie one of the earliest completed shots to a Death Star cannon model, giving the station real priority in the chronology.

Mid 1976 to late 1976

Battle Program

The shop now behaves like a production line with multiple miniature families moving at once.

1976 to 1977

X-wing heroes and pyros run as separate branches

The fighter line expands beyond hero models into a true destruction program with specialized high-finish and rougher pyro states.

Fighters Range
1976 to 1977

Y-wing and TIE families do the same

The same logic applies to Y-wings and TIEs: hero-like filming miniatures on one side, dedicated destruction stand-ins on the other.

Fighters Range
1976 to 1977

The Death Star works as an entire miniature ecosystem

Sphere, surface tiles, backwall lighting, gun towers, crane, silo pieces, and docking-bay structures all behave like linked systems.

Death Star Range
1976

The small Blockade Runner solves one specific opening-shot problem

A unique `16-inch` miniature is built to make the ship appear to recede farther than the motion-control track really allows.

Ships Range
1976

Sandcrawler becomes the shared-shop build

Huston remembers it as a model almost everyone touched, which makes it one of the clearest markers of the shop hitting full stride.

Ships Range
1976

At least two Landspeeder miniatures exist

The exact states stay open, but the count floor is now clearly above one apart from the full-scale vehicle.

Finish & Memory

The work turns into delivery pressure, then into evidence.

1976 to 1977

5757 preserves the day-to-day Van Nuys atmosphere

Dave Brown's footage gives us a human-scale record of the building, the workspaces, and the active crew environment.

Site Range
1976 to May 1977

Optical completion becomes the grind

Brown later recalls the printers running `24/7` and the final push going nearly right up to release.

Delivery Range
May 25, 1977

Star Wars is released

The first production window closes, even if crew memory suggests the actual finish line was only barely behind it.

American Cinematographer locks in a period account

Dykstra's technical article becomes one of the most valuable near-contemporary sources for timing, methods, and staffing.
