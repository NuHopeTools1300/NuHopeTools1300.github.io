Inspired by jcoffman99 and others, I have decided to share my journey into constructing a studio scale TIE fighter (target: ANH version) and will post links, data, and progress here. My motivation is similar: a lot of the data is not easy to access, there are countless dead links to images and defunct websites. There are apparently parts maps and discussions on another forum, but that has proven difficult to get access. I'm starting this at the part ID research and kit collection phase and aim to start sharing build progress early in the first quarter of 2025.

At present, my IDs are incomplete (see images in the part maps link) I have IDs for much of the exterior hull and wingstar hub; I've done the top and bottom "hatches" and front cockpit dash, but not yet made images (dreading doing the cockpit interior!). I'm updating these and posting them here:

Draft TIE Fighter part maps

Draft TIE Fighter donor kit list

As the links say, these are drafts and the maps are version-marked as "beta". Major-point "release" versions will then go into an RPF-hosted album and/or somewhere at least equally permanent. Comments, suggestions, hints, or outright IDs would be appreciated.

These lists would not have been possible without RPF, the awesome SciFiKitBash forum and all of the work done by folks who have painstakingly scanned and uploaded images of their kits. I am eternally in your debt!

I've updated the part map files for the wingstar hub and cockpit ball exteriors yesterday. I've also recently updated some enumeration errors and typos in the wingstar hub map. I've made some success identifying some of the parts I've seen queried in the past by other builders, so I thought I'd post some details of my current IDs here.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-10-29-at-11-39-22-png.1875658/]

The first is a dual rod #28: I believe this is part E1 from the Tamiya 1/25 Centurion with the ends trimmed off:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-10-29-at-11-42-39-png.1875660/]

The second part, #68, is I believe 275Y from the AMT 1/25 International Payhauler:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-10-29-at-11-45-52-png.1875661/]

Sorry I'm showing the reverse side of it relative to the TIE model (the scan on that side appears to be a bit less resolved).

Very nearly have IDs assigned to all wingstar hub parts (which isn't to say all the IDs are right). The last few stragglers are these usual suspects:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-10-29-at-12-02-56-png.1875663/]

I gave #14 its own thread. I haven't seen a SS tie that convincingly has this part right (sometimes for lack of images, sometimes because two or more models clearly conflict—unlikely to both be right).

#43 is a rectangular box with 6 peripheral rivet or fastener details. Somewhat non-descript and could be cut from the body or hull of a tank and I just keep missing it. One of my major weaknesses is spotting parts cut out of the surface of larger vehicle body panels!

#65 and 66 seem to be lights from an armoured vehicle. This style of covered light is common in IFVs and self-propelled howitzers. You see similar forward lights on Bradley FVs, but those weren't produced until 1981, and these parts were definitely on the ANH model. I've come across a few candidates, but nothing definitive. Nichimo Abbot is a possibility but I don't have a good scan.

Want to examine the hinge part of the rear hatch of the TIE, since some of the public information about this is either incomplete or unclear.

Below is the well-known part map that I believe originates from the Studio Scale Modeler's forum.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/d0f30a4d-591d-4421-af75-733d8f1a856f-jpg.969308/]

I agree with all the part IDs in here, most are straightforward and from very well-known kits. Part 11 can also be sourced from the M36 Jackson, as I believe this has the same sprue as the M10 and was available at the time. For similar reasons, I believe 16 can as well be sourced from the 1/48 King Tiger.

The outstanding parts I've identified as:

6: Tamiya 1/48 M60 US Medium Tank parts A10 & A11, cut to accommodate the end of part 11 in the figure.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-17-31-png.1877511/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-18-22-png.1877512/]

It's possible that other 1/48 M60 variants have this part, too. I haven't checked. An equivalent part can be found in the 1/35 kits, but they appear to be too big. They also differ in having four rivet details across the short margin, whereas the greebly on the TIE has three, just as in the 1/48. 

12: This one's a bit interesting because I think (I'm not sure) some models might have scratch-built this. Looking in closely:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-26-18-png.1877514/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-26-50-png.1877515/]

This appears to use a par cut down from one of the "saddle halves" of the Aurora 8" Howitzer: 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-30-09-png.1877516/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-30-18-png.1877517/]

I have to thank Studio Kitbash for pointing me to the Aurora Munitions Carrier in my other thread, which caused me to look at the 8" Howitzer in more detail.

That does leave a question about the part it is sitting on, highlighted here: 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-39-56-png.1877520/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2024-11-05-at-13-40-05-png.1877521/]

A little build update. Not much to show, but some progress on the basic hull form. Indeed, my holiday plans to get crackin' on this were thwarted by the tiny Mayku formbox I had access to, so the project shifted to homebrewed vacuum former. As I noted earlier, I'm doing this the way I think the ILM team did it. I'm also doing it because I learned from swgeek's thread that milling acrylic is a pain in the behind. The result will be an "inner" and "outer" sphere. The outer sphere will be the one that takes the scribe line details etc. The exposed bits of the inner sphere will be ground out after casting.

Here's the 5" acrylic hemisphere sitting on the new vacuum former in front of my oven, waiting for the first coat of 2mm styrene

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9592-3-jpg.1900749/]

Notice I've put the dome onto a 1cm-thick disc of plywood. The styrene is so thick that without this, you'd lose a lot of depth from the little 'draft slopes' that form invariably at the bottom. Your hull hemidomes wouldn't be hemispherical anymore and you'd get a weird-looking oblate hull.

To minimize this, I'm using a 6" PVC pipe connector. Sorry for the out-of-focus shot.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9593-3-jpg.1900750/]

I push it down over the hemisphere and quickly start the vacuum. The edge is fairly sharp, so you can really push the plastic in tight to the hemisphere.

Voilà!

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9594-3-jpg.1900751/]

Three layers of 2mm styrene over a 5" sphere gives you 5 1/4" pretty much bang on the money. However, I did sand down the perimeter of the acrylic hemisphere a little bit to take out some "wobble". I will need to check this with a height gauge later to make sure I didn't over do it.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9607-3-jpg.1900752/]

I leave the hemisphere inside and shave the styrene down close to it. I get it flush to the original dome with some sanding. I've marked the acrylic with Sharpie so I know when one side has gone too far. Here you can see me juuuuuust about to eff it up:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9608-3-jpg.1900754/]

All for now. Hopefully next update will be more interesting.

I had a bit of time to work on the top hatch this week. 

The main motivation for this post is that I recently discovered that many of the photos on the Propstore Auction site include camera and lens metadata in them. It makes it possible to correct lens distortion in Photoshop or Lightroom. I imported the corrected image to Illustrator and added some guides to align my Dymo tape lines:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9620-jpg.1903342/]

The result is a not-too-shabby start to a V1 for this part:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9621-jpg.1903344/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9623-jpg.1903346/]

I'm guessing I'll probably do it again (and again, and maybe again). But for now, I'll finish this all the way to the scribe lines and chipping stage and see how I feel about it.

This is my first foray into the world of this type of "industrial-design style" model-making, not having worked with styrene in this way before. Most of my previous model-making was a long time ago in a country far, far away and dealt mostly with natural history subjects, which are squishier and don't have a lot of straight lines—so they're more forgiving, even though accuracy matters there too.

I have been working on the rear hatch lately. This is one part that has always perplexed me; I've never been quite sure how it was made. So, I looked at as many photos as I could, took some time to think about it, did a few trials and this is the method I have come up with.

There has been some discussion about whether the middle of the impressed part of the cone is flat or domed. I agree that it is subtly domed, based on what I've seen in pictures. However, this could always be chalked up to lens distortion.

The main proof came from these pictures of parts apparently out of the original molds from ANH. This is the only internal view of the rear hatch I have ever seen:

[IMAGE: 20140422_195300.jpg]

A few things jumped out at me. The first obviously being the concave surface.The second thing is the putty around the window, which appears to have been put in before the window was cut out. So I suspect this was a feature of the master and not some funky molding technique. But, hey, I could be very wrong. Finally, there's the really sharp brim to this piece. Not like any of the other hatches.

So here's my best interpretation of how this component was made. 

I made a couple of cone bucks in Blender and 3D printed them to make a vacuum-forming buck (I can give you my STLs if you want). The following series of images are not really chronological and reflect a few different attempts at this.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9588-jpg.1917681/]

Then I trimmed out the cone with a height gauge:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9616-jpg.1917682/]

This would form the outer cone, which I then welded down to a dome that I vacuum-formed over my 5.25" hemidomes:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9609-jpg.1917683/]

To make the inner concavity, I felt from looking at images this surface may have been a bit concave, rather than conic, so I formed some 1.5mm styrene over a 2.75" hemisphere, then cut and sanded it down to the "right" diameters.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9710-jpg.1917684/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9711-jpg.1917685/]

This all fits together near-perfectly:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9717-jpg.1917686/]

Add another dome over the middle and the rosette of chips:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9720-jpg.1917687/]

I think the chips should taper inwards a bit; mine are probably a bit too parallel-sided. If I had thought it through a bit better, I would have formed them over the 2.75" hemisphere, too (perhaps immediately over the 1.5 mm pull). But here we are.

And here's the near-finished product:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9747-copy-jpg.1917688/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9744-jpg.1917689/]

Far from perfect, but happy enough with the result for now. I've learned a good few things that I would do differently if I were to do it all again. Overall, the process seems to work, but did require a good bit of puttying and sanding at the contact between the outer cone and the underlying dome. I don't want to do it again, but now when I look at it all I see are the imperfections! 
[IMAGE: yH5BAEAAAAALAAAAAABAAEAAAIBRAA7]

And, of course, in a nod to the original, I puttied the back:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_9748-jpg.1917690/]

Not really functional in my case, but does thicken out the window frame a bit, so why not.

Not a lot of build progress due to work, but now that things are settling down again I got the chance to work on the pylons. I've been puzzling over these for the last few months because the geometry is not easy to work out from reference alone. A shout-out to chazzychaz for sending me some measurements off his AMT TIE Fighter, which I used to check my measurements (rescaled) and things seem to check out.

Here's the main pylon roughed out.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_0023-jpeg.1943560/]

And here are the strut parts temporarily added into place:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_0028-jpeg.1943561/]

These all need some tidying up, but this is what I had time for today. I'll probably redo the strut parts that go top and bottom. For reasons I'll outline below, it's imperative that you get the measurements and geometry of these right. Once I'm happy with what I've got, I'll create a table of measurements and add it to the OP.

The first thing you might ask is: "why are you making the pylon as a single piece?" rather than making separate top and bottom halves and marrying them up along the hinge lines? Indeed, in an earlier effort I did just that. It turns out, I think the ILM model shop guys did them as one piece. These were then cast and cut in various ways to make top and bottom pylon halves, as well as the end pylons. 

For one, if you look at references, the mould seam doesn't actually line on the apex of the pylon, it's often just a little bit off:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2025-06-22-at-13-52-58-png.1943565/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2025-06-22-at-13-58-10-png.1943567/]

Instead, I think one master pylon was made, including 'top' and 'bottom' halves. This was cast, cut, and detailed in different ways depending on the component. The main one was made to fit the hull sphere, of course. Then, as is widely known, the terminal pylon was made by cutting the end off the main pylon.

What seems less widely known is that the leftover from that cut can be used to make the end plate for the wing. This is something I only just noticed in the last week:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/end_cap_cut-jpg.1943568/]

While I'm building my TIE using mostly "traditional" methods, I do use Blender to test out measurements and prototype components. Here's a quick screen grab to show the three pieces together:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2025-06-22-at-14-02-56-png.1943569/]

Indeed, if you look carefully at images of the end plate, you can even see the longitudinal grooves that you see on the main pylon (and to a lesser extend on the terminal pylon):

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2025-06-17-at-14-48-41-png.1943570/]

If you're going to do it the same way, then you need to get all the measurements very close to right, otherwise your end plate will look funny. The upshot is that this will help ensure that your terminal pylon and end plate have very similar footprints. This is important because they need to fit snugly between the greeblies on the wingstar hub and you want them to therefore have the same geometry. This is likely why the ILMers did it—they were clever like that!

For this second part about pylons and TIE hulls, I'm going to make a bit of a stretch. I'm not 100% certain, but this is what I'm leaning towards.

Normally in studio scale builds the top and bottom halves of the hull unit are built separately. They are then cast separately, each with a two-part mold, and the resulting parts assembled (none of this is a criticism of previous builds. That's the sensible way to do it when you're planning ahead a little bit. It's also how I first started this:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_0358-jpeg.1979227/]

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/untitled-2-jpg.1979228/]

In such a case, it would make the most sense for the parting line between the hull halves to simply follow the angle of the pylon. However, when we look at TIEs up close, that's not what we see. 

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/parting_line_tie-png.1979074/]

The parting line doesn't follow the angle of the face of the pylon, nor even the angle on paneling that lies on the body. This suggests that the mold of the top half of the ship wrapped just a tiny bit over the angle of the pylon. It's also notable how well the panelling aligns between top and bottom halves of the hull. In my opinion, this suggests two things. 1) The panelling was applied as a single piece to the hull; 2) the pylon was a single piece (rather than top + bottom pieces) when the mold was made. Since the parting line in the pylon lines up with the parting line of the hull, I suspect the pylon was attached to the hull as a whole part.

OK, how do we make a hollow casting from a mold covering only the outside of the master? One process would be "slush casting" or "rotational casting". Pour in some resin, rotate the mold so the resin coats all the surfaces; keep going until you hit the end of pot life and things start to gel to the inside. ILM didn't do that here. Photos show the insides of the TIE bodies are pretty smooth. Slush casting would leave irregular surfaces. It's not a great production option for a TIE, because you can't really control the thickness. So we can rule out a slush/rotaional casting.

Another option is this: 

They appear to have used this clay-up technique a lot (not sure how much lasagna was involved). X-Wings fuselage tops were definitely done this way; the master was carved from wood but the casts are hollow. There are a few photos circulating of this type of mold being prepared for Death Star tiles. I'll have a post later about the TIE wing star molds. (A bit of an aside: often, it seems the mold backs were rigid, rather than rubber. Possibly this was to enhance back-pressure for "biscuit resin" in order to facilitate "skinning").

So there it is, I think the TIE hull master was first molded in a fully assembled state (i.e. top and bottom halves). However, the two halves of the mold were split apart, and backing molds were made, probably by applying a thin, smoothed out layer of clay to the inside. This turned one mold effectively into two separate molds. 

If this was in fact the case, you would be able to see some evidence of this on the internal surfaces of hull casts. I've received some suggestion this is indeed the case, but time will tell. If you look at pylon castings, from what little you can see of the internal face, they are totally featureless:

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/screenshot-2025-10-18-at-17-39-45-png.1979233/]

(from here)

That's an end pylon (which I'll discuss in a later post). What you'll notice is that where the pylon joins the semicircular "cuff", there's no evidence of a join, there's no evidence of textural differences between the cuff and the arm of the pylon. Nothing. Maybe they cleaned it up real well with bondo and sanding. But that seems like a lot of effort for a surface you'll never see. One great way to get such a smooth finish would be to start with an open-backed mold, fill the cavity with clay, smooth it out a bit, and press the aluminum tube in to get an exact impression.

That's all for now. I'll finish up with a third part where I'll discuss why they might have done it this way, as well as some of the evidence that counts against my little theory.

Been a bit quiet on my part, but have been working on my TIE. Just re-built the top hatch which I'm much happier with now. Will start casting it this week.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_0786-jpeg.2018823/]

I wasn't quite happy with my original as there was some poor alignment between some areas that was bothering me. I find it more useful to try to aim for internal consistency than to try to get all the measurements exactly right. It's a bit like when you are drawing something: if you're too literal, it ends up not looking right.

To help, I used a carpenters profile gauge. These are cheap and can be found in most hardware stores.

[IMAGE: https://www.therpf.com/forums/attachments/img_0762-jpeg.2018828/]
