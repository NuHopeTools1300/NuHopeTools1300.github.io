It wasn't laziness.  

And I can assure you, they don't make models on a conveyor belt in China. That one almost made me spit my tea. :lol

I have written about this many times before, but I will do the condensed version as I don't feel like writing the same thing all over again...

Each Falcon model has about 1100 separate parts. Each model was built by hand by a small team of model-builders. I spent many weeks in China working with them over the years. Some of their model builders could run rings around us. They got mad-skilz :lol   

It is just that after they make so many of them, it is hard to keep up the quality. I am not defending that, I am just saying it is human nature. I know *I* wouldn't want to have to build and paint 1600 of these things for about $10 a day pay.

1600 models (including APs, R units, samples, etc) times 1100 = 1,760,00 individual parts that had to be hand assembled to get the run done.

I betcha that if YOU had to assemble 1.7 million parts, you might had a few flubs here and there. :lol Stuff happens.

Again, not defending them, your model should be PERFECT for the money you paid, I agree with that! In a perfect world, there should not be ANY defective product shipping out to customers. As a consumer, I am right there with ya!

I am just saying, that if you know anything about production engineering...statistically, there WILL be flawed units. It is a fact. EVERY company that produces large quantities of items overseas will have defective units. You just have to factor that the returns into your P&L.  

I have bought electronics products from multi-billion dollar companies (Like Sony and Kenwood) that were defective right out of the box. Does that mean they were "lazy"?

There are a million variables that the parent company (Like MR, or Sideshow, or Gentle Giant or Sony for that matter) cannot control with overseas manufacturing. How the product is produced on any given day...how it was handled....did it get bumped before the glue was dry?, what mood the QC guys were in that day, etc. ONE defective solder joint can kill an entire circuit of thousands of good joints.

Most of the factory workers in China probably haven't even SEEN Star Wars, and we want them to make a PERFECT copy of the Falcon as it appeared in ESB. That is really hard to do. It took about 18 months of research, planning, design, review, etc, to even get the models started.

I know the paint quality varied a great deal, but I understand why. I completely repainted/super-detailed my model and it took about 60 hours to get everything the way I wanted it. A factory CANNOT take 60 man-hours to paint each unit. (that would be 96,000 man-hours). They would go out of business and/or it would take 16 years to get them all done. Compromises have to be made or the models would just not get done. That is a fact of life. 

Yup. Some of the headlights are not working. That sucks. It shouldn't have happened. No excuse there. No idea what happened. I really have no idea how the mandibles would be crooked. The model was designed with an internal skeleton that runs throughout the structure to act as a support, so that nothing sags or moves. It may have taken a big jolt somewhere before it got to you. Maybe the freight company dropped that particular cargo container that day? I dunno.

I DO know...Everyone on THIS side of the ocean cared VERY, VERY much.

So, to just make blanket statements like..."It was laziness" or "It was the conveyor belt" or "Most of the models are defective" is just dumb.

Did you poll every one that owns a model and ask them if they were defective? THEN calculate the ratio of defectives to non-defectives, to determine that "most" of the models are bad?

Because 10 or 20 people say they have a problem, doesn't mean that MOST of the models are bad. I know 50 people who have perfect ones. (Mine is fine). That still leaves 1500+ models unaccounted for. 

And I still can't say that "most" of the models are perfect. I know there were very few returns, but even with THAT data, I cannot make a definitive, statistical statement as to the ratio of bad to good. There simply isn't enough data either way. People may have had defective units and just didn't feel like returning them. Too many variables. See what I mean?

I am just saying...you may want to avoid making claims with no real data to back it up. 

My .02 wupi-upi.

Okay, as you've asked here's my story.

This is my first Falcon.

It was a signed edition, bought off eBay from a dealer in the U.K.

Here's the first problem:

[IMAGE: falconprob.jpg]

This issue really bugged me, as I'd seen other member's birds without this issue - standard editions.

Below is my mandible issue. You can see (especially at the saucer's edge) the gaps between the edges of the mandibles and the nose are not equal.

This created an issue when the bird was viewed from most angles - and of course, once a problem is seen it's impossible to not see it. 

Saying all of this, as you can see the paint job on this bird was absolutely fantastic! But, stangely didn't feature any tiny paint splatter weathering.

The crooked placement of the 'deflector shield generators' (the two boxes either side of the nose, sitting upon both the saucer and mandible surface) bugged me also - but I'd seen this on other member's replicas.

[IMAGE: falconprob2.jpg]

I contacted MR and after a week or so the bird was collected and a replacement arrived. I'd like to add their customer service (via CardsInc IIRC) was excellent. Returning the faulty replica cost me absolutely zero!

My replacement arrived, and the first thing I noticed was 'AP' written upon the box!

I was really excited, opened the packaging (the AP documents and plaques had been removed - which I can understand) only to find an identical port side box placement problem as the signed edition I had returned!

I also like to say the paint job on the AP was nowhere near as good as the signed edition pictured above, although it did feature paint spatter weathering. 

Although the mandibles didn't exhibit an identical problem to the signed edition, they were *very slightly* out of line to each other vertically. 

I could live with that. 

However, upon closer inspection I found various issues with the model:

The engine deck upward facing fins were not completely painted.

The starboard docking ring had been placed out of centreline by a couple of millimeters.

A tiny detail part was missing from the port mandible edge - with a noticable break - I couldn't find the part in the packaging.

A box on the port rear quarter had been glued next to the gun ring crookedly, and a pipe had been bent to fit along the hull.

Where the cockpit tube joined the cockpit cone paint was missing, showing slight black strip.

All in all I was very unhappy with the replacement - but decided enough was enough and to go through the replacement sequence again was not worth the effort. I sold the replica on eBay - which was a very difficult thing to do, as I really wanted to retain the signature plaque as Ford's was the last autograph I wanted for my collection.

I realised I wouldn't get back what I had paid if I sold the bird without the signed plaque, and what would be the point of keeping it without the replica?

If I ever decide to buy another it would only be after personal inspection. It's also interesting to note I had two of the 'high end' and sought after editions, whereas other member's standard editions appear to be almost and in some cases, perfect! 

I believe adding an edition to your collection without major issues from the manufacturing process is down to luck, nothing more, nothing less. It's all about how the guy's glued the components together and painted them felt on the day. 

To sum up, the MR Falcon is an amazing piece, and we have many member's here to thank for bringing this wonderful ship into the homes of those who love it.

It's just unfortunate that at the end of the day, the people hired to build and finish these replicas are simply that - hired hands, employed to carry out a task, a job to pay their bills, and not build and finish these items with the same amount of love and dedication exhibited by those who developed it.

Theorizing about the mandible alignment issue in this old thread: Could it be a slight warp in the chassis/frame inside?

Someone more knowledgable could answer this question better - I don't know the details of how these were constructed - but, in theory, could it be possible that a batch of frames (the chassis's that everything hangs on) could've been warped somewhere along the way? during the production process?

A.)    Maybe, a stack waiting for assembly all drooped in the same subtle way due to heat, weight, or improper temporary storage, etc.

B.)    Or the mold, or jig for building the chassis/frame shifted slightly over time causing a slight offset

A slight discrepancy here could be hard to detect, but be amplified once the parts are assembled around it - and could cause the mandibles, or forks, to be off in the same way on repeated models.
